Bonds & precious metals — the ballast and the belief

Two asset classes people reach for when stocks scare them — for opposite reasons. A bond is a promise with a contract behind it; gold is a belief with 5,000 years behind it. This page explains both in plain words, shows the classic ways portfolios balance them, and hands you the questions those frameworks answer — the answers stay yours. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

Bonds — lending, turned into a thing you can own

What a bond is: a loan you can hold. You give a government or company money; they promise fixed interest payments (the coupon) and your money back on a set date (the maturity). Two things can happen to it: you hold to the end and collect exactly what was promised (if the borrower stays solvent), or you sell early at whatever price the market offers — and that price seesaws opposite to interest rates: when new bonds pay more, old ones paying less must sell cheaper. Same seesaw from our markets pack, now with your money on it.

Why portfolios include them — the four honest reasons:

1. A contract instead of a hope. A stock pays you whatever's left over after everyone else; a bond's payments are a legal obligation, first in line before shareholders get anything. Different legal species, not just a milder stock.

2. A different rhythm of risk. Bonds usually swing far less than stocks, and in many (not all) stock crashes, high-quality government bonds rose while stocks fell — the classic shock absorber.

3. Ballast you can act with. When stocks crash, a bond allocation is dry powder: rebalancing sells what held up and buys what fell — a rule that mechanically buys low without requiring courage or prediction.

4. Protection from bad timing at the worst age. See sequence-of-returns risk below — the retiree-specific danger bonds exist to blunt.

The types, from calmest promise to loudest

US Treasuries — the reference promise
Loans to the US government: bills (under a year), notes (2–10 years), bonds (20–30). Backed by the power to tax and print — the market treats their yield as the "risk-free rate," the ruler every other investment on Earth is measured against. That ruler is our Constraints force: when it moves, everything is re-measured.
TIPS & I-bonds — the inflation-proofed promise
Treasuries whose value adjusts with official inflation, attacking the one enemy regular bonds can't fight: money that repays in full but buys less. (Our macro gauge's DFII10 series is literally the TIPS yield — the market's live price of waiting, after inflation.)
Municipal bonds — loans to cities and states
Often tax-advantaged for US holders; default is rare but real (Detroit, Puerto Rico). The tax break is the product; read it against your own tax situation, which only you know.
Investment-grade corporate — loans to solid companies
Pay more than Treasuries because companies can fail. Rating agencies grade the risk (AAA down to BBB is "investment grade") — remembering that the same agencies famously mis-graded in 2008: a rating is an opinion with a good suit on.
High-yield ("junk") — loans to shaky companies
The loudest promise: big coupons because default is a live possibility. The honest catch: junk behaves like stocks exactly when you need bonds to behave like bonds — in a crisis it falls with everything else. Yield-chasing here is how "safe bond money" quietly becomes stock risk in disguise.
Funds vs. individual bonds vs. a ladder
A bond FUND owns thousands and never matures — smooth and simple, but its price floats forever with rates. INDIVIDUAL bonds held to maturity pay exactly the contract, whatever prices did in between. A ladder (bonds maturing in steps) is the household version: something matures regularly, no prediction needed. Neither is "better" — they answer different needs, which is the recurring theme of this whole page.
⚠ The half-truth that costs people: "bonds are safe." ▾ tap to open
The half-truth that costs people: "bonds are safe." The honest version: a high-quality bond's contract is safe (payments + principal at maturity, if the issuer stays solvent). Its price along the way is not — and duration measures how hard rates hit it. 2022 was the proof: as rates jumped from near zero, the broad US bond index fell about 13% — its worst year on record[11] — and long-dated Treasuries fell over 30%, a stock-crash-sized loss in the "safe" asset. People who owned long bonds thinking "safe" learned they'd been holding the seesaw's far end. Bonds manage risk; they do not delete it — nothing does.

What a risky portfolio actually looks like (any asset)

Risk isn't which assets you hold — it's the shape of the holding. The four shapes that wreck people, in every market on this project: concentration (one stock, one coin, one building = one story can end you) · leverage (borrowed size deletes your right to be temporarily wrong — the museum's every exhibit) · yield-chasing (reaching for the biggest payout without asking who pays it and why — Terra's 20% and junk bonds are the same question at different volumes) · and correlation faith (assuming your holdings won't all fall together; in real panics, they often do — LTCM's "uncorrelated" positions died as one). A portfolio can hold "safe" things in a risky shape.

The classic balances — described, not prescribed

60/40 — the century's default
60% stocks for growth, 40% bonds for ballast, rebalanced. The record: decades of smoother-than-stocks returns through most crashes — and one famous failure mode: 2022, when rising rates sank both sides at once (stocks −18%, bonds −13%) because the diversification depends on bonds zigging when stocks zag, which is usual, not guaranteed. Critics call it dated; defenders note it survived its obituary before. Both positions are on the record.
Age-in-bonds — the rule of thumb
Old form: hold your age as your bond percentage (a 30-year-old → 30% bonds). Modern variants say 110 or 120 minus age in stocks, reflecting longer lifespans. What it's really encoding: time to recover. A 25-year-old can wait out a decade-long crash; a 70-year-old may not get the decade. It's a clock turned into an allocation — crude on purpose, and honest about being crude.
The glide path / target-date idea
Start stock-heavy young, slide automatically toward bonds as a target year (usually retirement) approaches. Target-date funds automate the whole slide in one holding. The assumption underneath: risk capacity is mostly about when you'll need the money — which leads to the questions below.
The three-fund idea
Total US stocks + total international stocks + total bonds, low-cost, rebalanced — the Bogle-school answer: own everything on both sides of the balance, cheaply, and let the ratio (chosen by the questions below) do all the deciding. Its whole argument is that the RATIO is the only decision that matters much.

The questions the frameworks are answering — yours to answer, not ours

1. When will you need this money? ▾ tap to open

Every framework above is a canned answer to the same four personal questions. The frameworks differ; the questions don't:

1. When will you need this money? (the horizon) — money needed in 3 years and money needed in 30 live in different universes: the 30-year money can survive crashes the 3-year money can't. History's rough shape: the US stock market has had many terrible decades but, so far, dramatically fewer terrible 30-year stretches — while Japan's card in our record shows "so far" doing real work in that sentence.

2. How much loss can you survive — twice? Financially (would a 50% drop change your life?) and emotionally (would you sell at the bottom?). The second one is where most plans actually die: the scorekeeper data on investors underperforming their own funds is mostly people abandoning good plans at the worst moment.

3. What's the money's job? Grow for decades, preserve what exists, or pay you income now — three different jobs that point at different mixes, and one pot of money can be split into all three.

4. How long might you live? The uncomfortable one the industry handles bluntly: retirement money may need to work for 30+ years AFTER the paycheck stops — which means even retirees usually need some growth engine, and "all bonds at 65" quietly bets on a short life. Life expectancy isn't morbid here; it's the length of the racetrack.

⚠ The retiree's specific monster — sequence-of-returns risk — in one … ▾ tap to open
The retiree's specific monster — sequence-of-returns risk — in one tale of two retirees: both retire with the same pot, earn the same average return over 25 years, and withdraw the same amount yearly. Retiree A hits the crash in year 20; Retiree B hits the identical crash in year 1. A is fine; B can run out of money — because B was forced to sell shares cheap, early, to eat, and those sold shares never recovered for them. Same average, opposite endings: the ORDER of returns matters once withdrawals start. This — not fear of volatility itself — is the sober reason every classic framework gets bond-heavier near the spending years: bonds are the pantry you eat from during crashes so the growth engine is never sold at the bottom. It's the win-rate-vs-profit lesson wearing retirement clothes: averages hide the sequence, and the sequence is what ruins people.

Why bonds matter to this project specifically: the Treasury yield is the ruler — the "price of waiting" every asset on every page here is measured against. When it rises, future profits are worth less today (stocks re-price), gold's zero-yield costs more to hold, and crypto's no-yield speculation competes with a real paycheck for patience. That's the Constraints force on our dashboard, our FRED gauge, and the 10-yr positioning gauge in the markets pack — all one instrument pointed at the same ruler.

Precious metals — the belief asset, done honestly

The structural fact first ▾ tap to open

The structural fact first: gold pays nothing, builds nothing, and files no reports. There is no weighing machine under it — no earnings, no coupon, no cash to weigh. By this project's own split, gold lives on the voting-machine side with the movement markets… with one enormous difference: the vote has run for 5,000 years across every civilization, and gold has never gone to zero. It's the most respectable member of the no-scale family — belief, but belief with the longest unbroken track record any asset has ever produced. Both halves of that sentence are true at once, and honest gold-talk keeps them together.

What actually moves it: mostly two things. Fear (crisis, war, distrust of banks and currencies — gold is insurance against systems, not companies) and real interest rates (when safe bonds pay well after inflation, holding a metal that pays nothing costs you that paycheck; when real rates are negative, gold's zero yield suddenly beats a losing one). That second driver is measurable — it's literally our FRED gauge (DFII10) — and the gold-positioning gauge in the markets pack shows the crowd's lean. Rare for a belief asset: two of its main dials are on our dashboard already.

The case for a slice (as its advocates state it) ▾ tap to open

The case for a slice (as its advocates state it): it has historically zigged in some crashes when nearly everything zagged; it's insurance against currency debasement and system failure that no bond can offer (a bond is IN the system); and central banks themselves hold thousands of tons of it — the system's own managers keep some insurance against the system.

The case against (as the record states it): from its 1980 peak, gold lost roughly 70% of its purchasing power[12] and stayed down for twenty years — an entire investing lifetime of dead money, while stocks compounded; it pays nothing while you wait, so time is its enemy in a way time is a stockholder's friend; and holding it costs (storage, insurance, or fund fees) — negative yield on a zero-yield asset. Buffett's on-record dismissal is the classic: it just sits there, producing nothing, while farms grow food and businesses grow profits.

The classic compromise (described, not prescribed): the traditional folklore allocation is a small slice — often quoted at 5–10% — held explicitly as insurance, not investment: sized so its dead decades can't hurt much and its crisis days can help some. Critics answer that broad stocks-plus-bonds already carries you through most storms, and insurance you never need is just a fee. Both arguments are old, both are serious, and the sizing question routes straight back to the four questions above.

Silver — the hybrid
Half monetary metal, half industrial commodity (electronics, solar). It inherits gold's belief-driver AND factory demand — which makes it roughly twice as violent in both directions. Same family, louder cousin.
Platinum & palladium — mostly industry in a shiny costume
Overwhelmingly industrial demand (catalytic converters, chemistry). They trade more like commodities tied to the car industry than like belief assets — the "precious" label describes rarity, not role.
The three ways to hold — and how they differ more than people think
Physical (coins/bars): the pure version — no counterparty, but you pay a premium buying, storage/insurance holding, and a spread selling. Funds/ETFs: one tap, small yearly fee, but you hold a claim on gold in someone's vault — a promise-wrapper around the anti-promise asset, which some buyers find self-defeating and most find fine. Mining stocks: NOT gold at all — they're companies (with costs, debt, management, and 10-Qs you now know how to read) whose profits swing harder than the metal. Buying miners for "gold exposure" is buying leveraged businesses and calling it metal.
