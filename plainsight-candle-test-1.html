The candle test — crypto edition

The famous candle shapes all claim the same thing: they can tell you whether a gain or a dip comes next. This page checks that claim directly — every time each pattern appeared across ten major coins' real history, what did the next candle actually do, versus the base rate? Definitions and the scoring rule are printed before you run it. Honest scale note: the free data serves 4-day candles (~91 per coin, ~900 candles total) — patterns are tested at that rhythm and labeled so. Why not faster candles? Hourly and minute-by-minute price history — what most candlestick folklore actually claims to read — sits behind paid data licenses. This project is free forever, so we don't use paid sources; we use the most detailed history that's both free and legal. We're still looking for a free, legal source at finer resolution — if one exists, it goes in. Tap any underlined word for meanings.

The five questions this page answers — every test page asks the same five:
1 · What's being tested?The famous candlestick shapes — hammer, doji, engulfing, shooting star.
2 · What do they claim?That a specific shape tells you whether a gain or a dip comes next.
3 · How do we test it?Fixed pattern math printed before you run it, then the same four checks as the Signal Test, on ten coins' real candles fetched live.
4 · What did history show?Run it and see — including the honest answer to "but millions of people read candles."
5 · What can't this tell you?Anything about faster timeframes (that data isn't free and legal — stated plainly below), and never the future.

The pre-registered pattern math (so "I know it when I see it" can't rescue anything): body = |close−open| · range = high−low. Doji: body < 10% of range. Hammer: lower wick ≥ 2× body, upper wick ≤ body, after a falling candle. Shooting star: the mirror, after a rising candle. Engulfing: this body opens beyond and closes beyond the prior body, opposite color. Each pattern is graded on its own claim — bullish claims the next candle beats normal; bearish claims worse than normal; "reversal" claims the prior direction flips more often than it naturally does. Every pattern then faces the same four checks as the Signal Test — beat the base? beat a random dart? probably not luck (permutation)? survived testing several patterns at once? — and earns an evidence tier: STRONG / MODERATE / MIXED / LITTLE historical evidence (or INSUFFICIENT under 8 fires). Same checks, same margins, forever.

"No edge?? But millions of people read candles — what did this test do wrong?" The fairest question on the page, answered both ways. What this test genuinely can't see: intraday timeframes (free data serves 4-day candles; the folklore mostly lives at hours and minutes), and "context" or "confluence" use — which, as commonly taught, can't be pinned down enough to be wrong, which means it can't be right either. What this result is NOT: a fluke of ours — published academic studies testing candlestick patterns on decades of stock data with this same count-everything method reached the same answer: no reliable edge over the base rate. We replicated the literature, live, on your phone. Then why the millions? Brains are pattern-machines that find faces in clouds and hammers in noise; memory keeps hits and quietly drops misses; in a rising market ANY buy-shaped pattern "works" often enough to feel real (the base-rate trap — this page's deepest lesson); the surviving anecdotes spread while the failures stay silent; and an entire paid ecosystem earns only if the reading continues. None of that requires anyone to be lying — it only requires nobody counting. This page counts.

Where did the volume rules go? Removed honestly, two reasons: testing them required a second data request per coin, which tripped free rate limits and starved the whole test down to 3 coins — and at 4-day rhythm, 2× volume spikes fired 0–4 times a year, below any honest minimum. A pattern too rare to test at the rhythm we can see is itself a finding.

Why do people read candlesticks, then? WORTH UNDERSTANDING

Candlesticks are two different things wearing one name — and only one of them is being tested here. As a display format, they're genuinely excellent: one shape shows open, close, high, and low at a glance, which is why every professional chart on Earth uses them. That part isn't in question. What's tested on this page is the second thing — the claim that specific shapes predict what comes next — and the chart's real usefulness quietly lends the predictions borrowed credibility.

Why the predictions feel true even when they don't hold up: each pattern comes with a story ("sellers pushed, buyers won, the tide turned"), and stories feel convincing in a way plain statistics never do. Memory does the rest — the one hammer that called the exact bottom sticks; the dozen that didn't quietly fade. Almost nobody keeps score, which is exactly why a page like this one is unusual instead of routine.

Why crypto specifically: with no earnings or filings to weigh, chart reading fills the gap as the market's shared language. The loudest teachers are often exchanges and course-sellers who earn on trading VOLUME, not on being right — worth remembering while reading anyone's "setup."

And the fair steelman: if enough traders watch the same shape and act on it together, price can move a little just because they believed it — a weak, real effect researchers call reflexivity. A few patterns also loosely track genuine short-term tendencies. So the tradition isn't empty; it's mostly a compelling story wrapped around a couple of small effects, which is what the numbers above are measuring with the story removed.

Two things this market structurally teaches (no test needed): crypto has no gap patterns — gaps are the space between yesterday's close and today's open, and a market that never closes cannot have them; every "gap trading" framework imported from stocks is describing a thing that doesn't exist here. And spot currency volume doesn't exist anywhere: forex has no central exchange, so no true volume number is published at any price — "forex volume indicators" measure broker-local samples dressed as the world. When a sold tool claims to read a number nature doesn't produce, that's the whole review.
🪞 The mirror — our self-check ▾ tap to open
What "the mirror" means (it ends every room): after testing everyone else's claims, we turn the same test on ourselves — in two words: self-check. Three questions, every time: what did this page actually prove, what can it NOT know, and did it ever slip from showing you what IS into telling you what to DO? A page anywhere else that never holds a mirror to itself is telling you something too.
The mirror, as always: these verdicts are history, not prophecy — patterns that failed here could luck out tomorrow; ones that held could be traded away. Nothing here says buy, sell, or wait. What's permanent is the method you just watched: take the claim literally, count every case, compare to doing nothing. Any pattern anyone ever sells you can be put through that same door — and now you own the door. Data: CoinGecko, read live; recomputed fresh on every run.
