The case for active — at full strength

Our own pages preach the scorekeeper verdict: most active managers lose to the index. All true. But a fair court hears the defense at full volume, and the defense has real evidence — starting with the crime the prosecution glosses over. This page makes the strongest honest case for active management, every claim sourced, and closes with the counter-case, because that's the house rule. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

⚖️ The indictment — buy-and-hold's buried failure mode

"The market always recovers" is true for the index and false for the person. The index is immortal; you are not. The recovery statistics that comfort everyone are measured over the market's lifetime — but you only get YOUR window, and history's windows include these:

Japan, 1989: a saver who came into money at the peak — an inheritance, a retirement lump sum, a business sale — waited 35 years for the index to break even. A 55-year-old was 90. Most were dead. "Long run" arrived after the lifetime it was promised to serve.

United States, 1929: the Dow did not regain its peak until November 1954 — 25 years. (Dividends and deflation soften the real-terms picture to roughly a decade and a half by most counts — still most of a saving career.)

United States, 1966: no single crash, just a grinding stretch — roughly a decade and a half of negative real returns, with inflation doing the damage a crash usually does, invisibly. A 1966 retiree following "just hold" watched buying power bleed for most of their remaining life.

The honest name for this: cohort risk — your lifetime results depend enormously on WHEN you were born and when your money arrived, which you did not choose and cannot diversify. "Time in the market" quietly assumes you have the time, and got a survivable start date. For the wrong cohorts, buy-and-hold wasn't a strategy that recovered — it was a generational loss with no insurance attached. Our own sequence-of-returns section proves the mechanism; this page lets the implication land: passive's worst case is catastrophic, uninsured, and assigned by birth-year lottery. The strongest case for active management is that it is the attempt to buy that missing insurance.

📜 Exhibit A — a century of trend-following (the crown jewel)

If active management's defense had one exhibit, it's this. Trend-following — the rule "hold what's been rising, exit or reverse what's been falling," applied mechanically across many markets — has the deepest evidence base of any active strategy ever tested:

Positive average returns in EVERY decade since 1880. Not most decades — every one, across 59 markets (stocks, bonds, currencies, commodities), through war and peace, inflation and deflation, high and low rates. And the result survives simulated transaction costs AND hedge-fund-sized fees (2-and-20).
Hurst, Ooi & Pedersen (AQR), "A Century of Evidence on Trend-Following Investing" — peer-published, 137 years of data.
Positive in 8 of the 10 largest 60/40 drawdowns in 137 years. Read that against the indictment above: in the exact generational disasters where buy-and-hold's cohorts drowned, the mechanical trend rule usually MADE money — earning the industry name "crisis alpha." The most recent real-time test: 2022, when stocks and bonds fell together and the 60/40 had one of its worst years ever, trend funds broadly posted strong gains — live, out of sample, in front of everyone.
Same paper, Exhibit 6; SocGen CTA Trend Index for the 2022 live result.
It isn't a statistical fluke or a paywalled secret. The momentum effect has been documented across two centuries of data by independent academics, exists in nearly every asset class ever tested, and the managed-futures industry running it holds hundreds of billions — a strategy that has survived publication, decades of live money, and academic attack. That's the profile of a real phenomenon, not a backtest mirage.
Geczy & Samonov (two centuries of momentum); Moskowitz, Ooi & Pedersen (time-series momentum); industry AUM per CTA industry data.

📉 Exhibit B — even crude timing rules bought the insurance

The 10-month moving average test[8]: one of the most-downloaded investment papers ever simply asked: hold each asset class only when it's above its 10-month average; sit in cash otherwise. Result over 1901–2012, across five asset classes: equity-like returns with bond-like volatility and drawdowns — and it largely sidestepped the 1930s and the 2000s, the two worst cohort-traps in US history. Jeremy Siegel — the patron saint of "stocks for the long run" himself — ran the 200-day version on the Dow since 1900 and found improved risk-adjusted returns even after all costs (absolute returns slightly short of holding).
Faber, "A Quantitative Approach to Tactical Asset Allocation" (SSRN); Siegel, Stocks for the Long Run, as cited therein.

Read Exhibits A and B carefully and notice what they do NOT claim: neither reliably beat buy-and-hold's raw returns. What they demonstrably bought, across a century-plus of data, was the truncation of the left tail — smaller maximum drawdowns, survivable worst cases. Which is precisely the thing the indictment says buy-and-hold fatally lacks. The strongest case for active isn't "beat the market." It's "never be the 1989 Japanese retiree" — and the evidence says systematic rules have historically delivered exactly that trade: a slice of upside exchanged for insurance against the generational wipeout. Insurance always costs premium; the question was never whether it costs, but whether the catastrophe it covers is real. The indictment shows it's real.

🏆 Exhibit C — the existence proofs

The efficient-market case says nobody can beat the market except by luck. The record contains verified counterexamples that luck cannot plausibly explain (the lottery-steelman math from our Thorp deep-dive): Thorp — audited, near-two-decades, tiny swings, mechanism stated in advance. Renaissance's Medallion fund — the most documented[9]: roughly 66% gross (39% net) annually for some thirty years, a compounding record chance essentially cannot fake at that sample size. Buffett — a five-decade public record built on reading filings. And Schwager's interviewed winners, whose one shared trait was obsessive loss control. Markets are beatable — this is no longer scientifically controversial. The honest dispute is only about by whom, at what scale, and whether the door stays open once found.

🧠 Exhibit D — the theory says active MUST work for someone

This is the defense's deepest argument, and it comes from the academy itself: the Grossman–Stiglitz paradox (1980)[10] proved that perfectly efficient markets are logically impossible — prices only become right because active traders are paid to make them right. If active management truly earned nothing, everyone would index, prices would drift from reality, and picking would become profitable again. Active returns are the wage the market pays for its own accuracy — the wage can be competed thin, but it cannot be zero. Add the behavioral argument: the momentum and panic patterns that active systems harvest exist because of human fear, greed, and slow updating — and human nature has no product cycle. The anomalies documented across two centuries persist because their cause does.

🔧 Exhibit E — the historic objections have weakened

The classic retail-active studies (Barber–Odean's losing day-traders) were run in an era of fat commissions, wide spreads, and paywalled data. Today: commissions near zero, index-fund-grade costs on systematic strategies, spreads tightened, and the raw materials — every filing, every price, the COT report, EDGAR — free to anyone (this project is proof). The cost headwind that ate the historic retail active trader is a fraction of what it was. The defense doesn't claim this makes discretionary trading wise — it claims the rule-based, low-cost, diversified forms of active (the only forms the evidence above supports) are now implementable by ordinary people for close to nothing, which was never true before this generation.

🧾 The defense rests — what it actually established

Assembled honestly, the strongest case for active management is NOT the day-trader's case. It is: (1) buy-and-hold carries an uninsured, birth-lottery catastrophe risk that its marketing understates; (2) a century-plus of peer-reviewed evidence shows mechanical trend/timing rules historically truncated exactly that catastrophe, at the cost of some upside — insurance, priced as insurance; (3) verified winners exist, so "impossible" is false; (4) theory guarantees active returns exist for someone, because they're the market's wage for accuracy; (5) the costs that once made retail implementation hopeless have collapsed. That is a serious, evidence-grounded position held by serious researchers — not cope, not a scam, not survivorship folklore.

The counter-case, as the house rules require: most people who attempt active management fail — the scorekeepers are unanimous (~92%[5] of US funds behind their benchmarks over 20 years; 93% of Indian retail derivatives accounts losing; Barber–Odean's day-traders). The trend evidence's own author published the sequel honestly: the 2010s were a muted decade for trend, and simple timing rules typically cost some absolute return (the lag is the premium). Every insurance strategy underperforms most of the time BY DESIGN — and most humans abandon it during the underperformance, right before it pays, which is how a good system produces bad results in real hands. And note what the defense's own exhibits are: boring, mechanical, diversified rules held for decades — the winning form of "active" looks almost nothing like what the word means to a person with a phone and a leverage slider. The two camps converge more than either admits: both say costs kill, discipline decides, prediction fails, and survival is the game. Where they truly differ is one question — whose catastrophe do you fear more: the drawdown you hold through, or the one you mistime your exit from? Honest people answer differently. Now you have both cases at full strength.
