Citations

Every number you see linked as [1], [2], etc. anywhere in this app leads here. Each entry names the real source, states plainly what was actually checked and when, and links back to every page that cites it. This page exists because "we checked" isn't good enough — you should be able to see what we checked.

How to read the tags: live-verified = read directly from the source's own live system by this app, reproducible by you right now. search-verified = confirmed against the named primary source this session; not a live feed, so treat it as dated to when we checked. license-verified = a data source's usage terms were read before we built on it. training-recall = a stable, widely-replicated finding we state directionally and did not re-verify this session — the weakest tier here, labeled so you can weigh it accordingly.

Sources named in full

[1] SEBI (India) — retail derivatives studysearch-verified
Securities and Exchange Board of India, study released ~September 2024, covering trading years FY22–FY24: found roughly 93% of individual futures-and-options traders lost money over the period, with about 1% earning meaningful profit after costs. This is a regulator's own account-level count, not a survey or industry estimate — the strongest kind of evidence this app cites anywhere.
Cited on: The remaining markets compendium
[2] ESMA — retail CFD account outcomessearch-verified
European Securities and Markets Authority, standardized disclosures brokers are required to publish under EU rules: 74–89% of retail CFD accounts lose money, varying by broker and period. Regulator-mandated, standardized methodology across firms.
Cited on: The remaining markets compendium · Markets pack
[3] Retail options purchase returns & 0DTE volumesearch-verified
Academic market-microstructure research (Bogousslavsky & Muravyev; Beckmeyer, Branger & Gayda) measuring average retail option trade returns (purchases averaging roughly −4% at the moment of the trade) and same-day-expiry ("0DTE") option volume and losses, estimated in the hundreds of thousands of dollars per day in aggregate. Independent academic papers, not industry-published.
Cited on: The remaining markets compendium
[4] Cboe — retail 0DTE structure studysearch-verified
Cboe Global Markets' own published research on retail zero-day-to-expiry option trades, finding a large majority use defined-risk structures (known maximum loss at entry). Exchange-published — a real data point, disclosed here as exchange-sourced rather than independent, since Cboe profits from options volume.
Cited on: The remaining markets compendium
[5] SPIVA — active fund performance vs. benchmarkssearch-verified
S&P Dow Jones Indices' SPIVA scorecard: approximately 92% of US stock funds underperformed their benchmarks over the 20 years through 2025 (updated from an earlier ~94% figure this app carried in prior sessions — corrected after re-verification). Methodology dispute disclosed: a 2026 industry-sponsored study argues SPIVA's counting method (treating every closed fund as a loser, weighting small funds equally) overstates the failure rate; SPIVA and independent reviewers defend the method. Even the dispute's friendliest reading still finds no reliable way to pick winning funds in advance.
Cited on: Markets pack · How to read a company · Remaining markets · Wealth bookshelf · Case for active
[6] Barber & Odean — individual day-trader outcomessearch-verified
Peer-reviewed study of the full population of day traders on the Taiwan Stock Exchange: roughly 80% lost money before costs were even considered in some samples; under 1% were consistently profitable after fees; the most active traders performed worst. A whole-market account-level study, not a survey.
Cited on: Wealth bookshelf
[7] AQR — "A Century of Evidence on Trend-Following Investing"search-verified
Hurst, Ooi & Pedersen (AQR Capital Management): trend-following strategies showed positive average returns in every decade from 1880 to the present across 59 markets, and were positive in 8 of the 10 largest 60/40 portfolio drawdowns over 137 years, net of simulated costs and fees. AQR later published a follow-up ("You Can't Always Trend When You Want," 2019) disclosing a muted decade for trend strategies in the 2010s — both papers from the same source are cited, not just the favorable one.
Cited on: The case for active
[8] Faber — "A Quantitative Approach to Tactical Asset Allocation"search-verified
Mebane Faber's widely-cited paper testing a simple 10-month moving-average rule across five asset classes from 1901–2012, finding equity-like returns with meaningfully smaller drawdowns than buy-and-hold. Jeremy Siegel independently tested a 200-day version on the Dow back to 1900 in his own published work and found improved risk-adjusted (though not absolute) returns after costs.
Cited on: The case for active
[9] Renaissance Technologies' Medallion Fundsearch-verified
Widely reported (notably in Gregory Zuckerman's "The Man Who Solved the Market") returns of roughly 66% gross / 39% net annually over approximately 30 years — an internal fund closed to outside investors, so figures come from investor disclosures and reporting rather than public audited filings. Flagged here as strongly-sourced but not equivalent to a regulator's own count.
Cited on: The case for active
[10] Grossman & Stiglitz (1980) — the impossibility of informationally efficient marketssearch-verified
A foundational, peer-reviewed economics paper proving that perfectly efficient markets are logically self-defeating: if prices reflected all information, nobody would be paid to gather it, so nobody would, and prices would stop reflecting it. Cited here as established economic theory, not as evidence any particular strategy or person profits.
Cited on: The case for active
[11] 2022 US bond market drawdownsearch-verified
Widely reported market data: the broad US investment-grade bond index fell approximately 13% in 2022 — its worst calendar year on record — while long-dated US Treasuries fell more than 30%, driven by the fastest interest-rate increases in decades. Cross-checkable against any major bond index provider's public historical return data.
Cited on: Bonds & metals
[12] Gold, 1980–2000 real returnsearch-verified
Widely reported: gold's price, adjusted for inflation, fell roughly 70% from its January 1980 peak and did not recover for two decades — a commonly cited case study in commodities and precious-metals literature, cross-checkable against any long-run gold price series and US inflation data.
Cited on: Bonds & metals
[13] Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) — leverage and rescuesearch-verified
Federal Reserve Bank historical accounts: LTCM's leverage ratio rose from roughly 28:1 to over 100:1 as its capital was destroyed in 1998; the Federal Reserve organized (without public money) 14 banks to inject $3.6 billion to unwind the fund's positions in an orderly way, citing systemic risk to the broader financial system. Sourced to the Federal Reserve's own historical record.
Cited on: The living museum of losses
[14] CAPE (Shiller P/E) — July 2026 level and December 1999 recordsearch-verified
Shiller's cyclically-adjusted price-earnings ratio for the US stock market, reported at approximately 40–42 as of July 2026 against a long-run median near 16 and an all-time recorded high of 44.2 in December 1999 (just before the dot-com crash). Sourced to Shiller's own published CAPE series.
Cited on: Who called it
[15] Irving Fisher, October 16, 1929search-verified
Fisher's "permanently high plateau" remark was reported contemporaneously in the New York Times (October 16, 1929), roughly two weeks before the crash began; his personal financial ruin from margin borrowing is part of the historical record covered in Federal Reserve historical accounts of the period. Quote kept under 15 words per this app's copyright discipline.
Cited on: Who called it
[16] Nomura — 1989 Nikkei 80,000 projectionsearch-verified
A 1989 research projection from Japan's largest brokerage forecasting the Nikkei index (then near 39,000) would reach 80,000 by 1995; referenced in later academic and journalistic reviews of the Japanese asset bubble as a contemporaneous example of extrapolative forecasting near a market peak.
Cited on: Who called it

The 1999 book's central argument and a contemporaneous academic review (by economist N. Gregory Mankiw) calling the underlying mechanism "not as laughable as it sounds" while rejecting the confidence of the timeline. The Dow's actual crossing of 36,000 in November 2021 is a matter of public market record.
Cited on: Who called it
[18] Shiller & Campbell — December 1996 warning to the Federal Reservesearch-verified
Robert Shiller (Yale) and John Campbell (Harvard) presented valuation-based warnings about US stock market levels to the Federal Reserve in December 1996, with the Dow near 6,437 — an account recorded in later academic writing, including Mankiw's own retrospective. The market roughly doubled over the following three years before the 2000 crash.
Cited on: Who called it
[19] "Bitcoin obituaries" — aggregate trackersearch-verified
A community-maintained public list (not a peer-reviewed study) cataloguing hundreds of on-record declarations that Bitcoin is "dead" since 2010. Cited explicitly as a popular count, not as academic evidence — flagged this way on the page itself.
Cited on: Who called it
[20] Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI) — U.S. Securities and Exchange Commissionsearch-verified
SEC rule that took effect in 2019, raising the standard for broker-dealers recommending securities to retail customers above the older "suitability" standard, while stopping short of the full fiduciary duty that registered investment advisers owe. Primary record: sec.gov.
Cited on: Getting help
[21] Bernard Madoff — self-custody of client assetssearch-verified
Well-documented in SEC enforcement records and the criminal case: Madoff's firm acted as its own custodian, issuing account statements from the same operation running the fraud — a structural condition regulators and advisers now specifically warn investors to avoid. Primary record: SEC litigation releases, sec.gov.
Cited on: Getting help

The CFP Board's own published standards: a qualifying exam, a bachelor's degree, relevant experience requirements, and an ongoing fiduciary duty when the certificant is providing financial advice. Primary record: CFP Board's public standards pages.

[28] Morningstar — "Mind the Gap" investor-return studysearch-verified
Morningstar's annual study comparing what funds earned with what the average invested dollar in them earned: over the decade ended December 31, 2024, investors' dollars earned about 7.0% per year while their funds returned 8.2% — a 1.2-point yearly gap (~15% of returns), consistent across every rolling period measured. Dispute disclosed, our standard practice: a 2024–26 academic critique (Fulkerson, Jordan, Riley & Yan) argues that poor timing per se explains only ~0.10%/yr of the gap, with the rest arising from mechanical factors in how the gap is computed. The gap itself is measured fact; how much of it is "bad behavior" is genuinely contested — our behavior room says so on its face.
Cited on: The behavior room
[29] Lump sum vs. dollar-cost averagingtraining-recall — cross-checkable
Widely replicated finding (Vanguard's well-known study and successors): investing an available lump sum immediately has historically beaten spreading it in over months roughly two-thirds of the time, simply because markets rise more often than they fall. Tiered honestly as training-recall: we did not re-verify the precise percentage this session, and our Start Here page therefore states the direction ("most of the time") rather than a decimal. Cross-checkable against Vanguard's published research.
Cited on: Start here

What this page is not: a promise that every sentence in the app traces to a numbered source — plain arithmetic, mechanism explanations ("how a bond works"), and the app's own live-measured readings (the board, the pack, the tests) don't need external citation because they ARE the primary source, shown working. This registry covers the claims that borrow from the outside world: named studies, regulators, historical events, and licensed datasets.

🪞 The mirror — our self-check ▾ tap to open
What "the mirror" means (it ends every room): after testing everyone else's claims, we turn the same test on ourselves — in two words: self-check. Three questions, every time: what did this page actually prove, what can it NOT know, and did it ever slip from showing you what IS into telling you what to DO? A page anywhere else that never holds a mirror to itself is telling you something too.
The mirror, for a page that cites sources: a citation isn't proof by itself — it's a pointer you can go check. This registry can be wrong (a source can be misread, a link can rot, a study can be superseded), and the honest fix isn't to trust it more, it's to click through. The tier tags exist so you know exactly how hard we checked each one — and training-recall entries are flagged as the weakest tier on purpose. If a citation here ever looks thin, that's worth telling us.
