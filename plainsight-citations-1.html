Citations

Every number you see linked as [1], [2], etc. anywhere in this app leads here. Each entry names the real source, states plainly what was actually checked and when, and links back to every page that cites it. This page exists because "we checked" isn't good enough — you should be able to see what we checked.

How to read the tags: live-verified = read directly from the source's own live system by this app, reproducible by you right now. search-verified = confirmed against the named primary source this session; not a live feed, so treat it as dated to when we checked. license-verified = a data source's usage terms were read before we built on it. training-recall = a stable, widely-replicated finding we state directionally and did not re-verify this session — the weakest tier here, labeled so you can weigh it accordingly.

Sources named in full

[1] SEBI (India) — retail derivatives studysearch-verified
Securities and Exchange Board of India, study released ~September 2024, covering trading years FY22–FY24: found roughly 93% of individual futures-and-options traders lost money over the period, with about 1% earning meaningful profit after costs. This is a regulator's own account-level count, not a survey or industry estimate — the strongest kind of evidence this app cites anywhere.
Cited on: The remaining markets compendium
[2] ESMA — retail CFD account outcomessearch-verified
European Securities and Markets Authority, standardized disclosures brokers are required to publish under EU rules: 74–89% of retail CFD accounts lose money, varying by broker and period. Regulator-mandated, standardized methodology across firms.
Cited on: The remaining markets compendium · Markets pack
[3] Retail options purchase returns & 0DTE volumesearch-verified
Academic market-microstructure research (Bogousslavsky & Muravyev; Beckmeyer, Branger & Gayda) measuring average retail option trade returns (purchases averaging roughly −4% at the moment of the trade) and same-day-expiry ("0DTE") option volume and losses, estimated in the hundreds of thousands of dollars per day in aggregate. Independent academic papers, not industry-published.
Cited on: The remaining markets compendium
[4] Cboe — retail 0DTE structure studysearch-verified
Cboe Global Markets' own published research on retail zero-day-to-expiry option trades, finding a large majority use defined-risk structures (known maximum loss at entry). Exchange-published — a real data point, disclosed here as exchange-sourced rather than independent, since Cboe profits from options volume.
Cited on: The remaining markets compendium
[5] SPIVA — active fund performance vs. benchmarkssearch-verified
S&P Dow Jones Indices' SPIVA scorecard: approximately 92% of US stock funds underperformed their benchmarks over the 20 years through 2025 (updated from an earlier ~94% figure this app carried in prior sessions — corrected after re-verification). Methodology dispute disclosed: a 2026 industry-sponsored study argues SPIVA's counting method (treating every closed fund as a loser, weighting small funds equally) overstates the failure rate; SPIVA and independent reviewers defend the method. Even the dispute's friendliest reading still finds no reliable way to pick winning funds in advance.
Cited on: Markets pack · How to read a company · Remaining markets · Wealth bookshelf · Case for active
[6] Barber & Odean — individual day-trader outcomessearch-verified
Peer-reviewed study of the full population of day traders on the Taiwan Stock Exchange: roughly 80% lost money before costs were even considered in some samples; under 1% were consistently profitable after fees; the most active traders performed worst. A whole-market account-level study, not a survey.
Cited on: Wealth bookshelf
[7] AQR — "A Century of Evidence on Trend-Following Investing"search-verified
Hurst, Ooi & Pedersen (AQR Capital Management): trend-following strategies showed positive average returns in every decade from 1880 to the present across 59 markets, and were positive in 8 of the 10 largest 60/40 portfolio drawdowns over 137 years, net of simulated costs and fees. AQR later published a follow-up ("You Can't Always Trend When You Want," 2019) disclosing a muted decade for trend strategies in the 2010s — both papers from the same source are cited, not just the favorable one.
Cited on: The case for active
[8] Faber — "A Quantitative Approach to Tactical Asset Allocation"search-verified
Mebane Faber's widely-cited paper testing a simple 10-month moving-average rule across five asset classes from 1901–2012, finding equity-like returns with meaningfully smaller drawdowns than buy-and-hold. Jeremy Siegel independently tested a 200-day version on the Dow back to 1900 in his own published work and found improved risk-adjusted (though not absolute) returns after costs.
Cited on: The case for active
[9] Renaissance Technologies' Medallion Fundsearch-verified
Widely reported (notably in Gregory Zuckerman's "The Man Who Solved the Market") returns of roughly 66% gross / 39% net annually over approximately 30 years — an internal fund closed to outside investors, so figures come from investor disclosures and reporting rather than public audited filings. Flagged here as strongly-sourced but not equivalent to a regulator's own count.
Cited on: The case for active
[10] Grossman & Stiglitz (1980) — the impossibility of informationally efficient marketssearch-verified
A foundational, peer-reviewed economics paper proving that perfectly efficient markets are logically self-defeating: if prices reflected all information, nobody would be paid to gather it, so nobody would, and prices would stop reflecting it. Cited here as established economic theory, not as evidence any particular strategy or person profits.
Cited on: The case for active
[11] 2022 US bond market drawdownsearch-verified
Widely reported market data: the broad US investment-grade bond index fell approximately 13% in 2022 — its worst calendar year on record — while long-dated US Treasuries fell more than 30%, driven by the fastest interest-rate increases in decades. Cross-checkable against any major bond index provider's public historical return data.
Cited on: Bonds & metals
[12] Gold, 1980–2000 real returnsearch-verified
Widely reported: gold's price, adjusted for inflation, fell roughly 70% from its January 1980 peak and did not recover for two decades — a commonly cited case study in commodities and precious-metals literature, cross-checkable against any long-run gold price series and US inflation data.

[13] Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) — leverage and rescuesearch-verified

Sourced to the Federal Reserve's own historical record.

[28] Morningstar — "Mind the Gap" investor-return studysearch-verified
Morningstar's annual study comparing what funds earned with what the average invested dollar in them earned: over the decade ended December 31, 2024, investors' dollars earned about 7.0% per year while their funds returned 8.2% — a 1.2-point yearly gap (~15% of returns), consistent across every rolling period measured. Dispute disclosed, our standard practice: a 2024–26 academic critique (Fulkerson, Jordan, Riley & Yan) argues that poor timing per se explains only ~0.10%/yr of the gap, with the rest arising from mechanical factors in how the gap is computed. The gap itself is measured fact; how much of it is "bad behavior" is genuinely contested — our behavior room says so on its face.

Cross-checkable against Vanguard's published research.

The methods behind our own testing

Everything above is a source for a claim the app makes. This section is different: these are the sources for how we test — the published methods our signal test is built from, and the published methods for checking that a test is itself honest. Added S22 after an adversarial audit found real errors in our own statistics. If you want to check our work, or grade whether we are doing this properly, start here. All entries below were search-verified in July 2026.

[34] López de Prado — "Advances in Financial Machine Learning" (Wiley, 2018)search-verified
The standard modern reference on not fooling yourself with a backtest. Argues you are almost guaranteed to overfit even when you are trying not to, and that a high Sharpe ratio means nothing until you know how likely it was to be luck. Introduces purged and embargoed cross-validation: ordinary cross-validation breaks a rule that matters enormously in finance — you must not train on the future — and overlapping observations leak the answer backwards. This is the same overlap problem our own S22 audit found in our permutation test (clustered fires inside overlapping outcome windows), arrived at independently and confirmed here in the published literature. Also the source of the Deflated Sharpe Ratio and the Probability of Backtest Overfitting.
Method source for: The signal test · the calibration lab
[35] Bailey & López de Prado — "The Deflated Sharpe Ratio" (Journal of Portfolio Management, 2014, 40(5):94–107)search-verified
Corrects a performance number for two things that inflate it: selection bias from testing many strategies, and returns that are not bell-curve shaped. Directly relevant to our custom-rule builder, where a user tries rule after rule until one looks good — the same problem, and the reason we now correct by attempt count.
Method source for: the custom-rule builder's multiple-testing correction
[36] Bailey, Borwein, López de Prado & Zhu — "Pseudo-Mathematics and Financial Charlatanism" (Notices of the American Mathematical Society, 2014, 61(5):458–471)search-verified
A mathematics-society paper arguing that backtests published without disclosing how many variations were tried are effectively meaningless, and that this is widespread in the industry. Freely readable online. Probably the single best short piece for a non-specialist on why "look at this amazing backtest" deserves suspicion.
Method source for: The signal test · What we've learned
[37] Bailey, Borwein, López de Prado & Zhu — "The Probability of Backtest Overfitting" (Journal of Computational Finance, 2017, 20(4):39–70)search-verified
Gives an actual number for how likely it is that a strategy's backtest success came from over-tuning rather than a real effect, using combinatorially symmetric cross-validation. Not yet implemented in our engine — listed here as a known gap, not as something we do.
Not yet implemented — logged as an open method gap
[38] Harvey, Liu & Zhu — "…and the Cross-Section of Expected Returns" (Review of Financial Studies, 2016, 29(1):5–68)search-verified
The paper behind the famous higher bar: because hundreds of predictors have been tested across the literature, the usual t≈2 standard is far too easy, and a newly claimed factor should clear roughly t>3. States plainly that most claimed research findings in financial economics are likely false. Important caveat we apply to ourselves: their t>3 is the OUTPUT of a model given ~316 tested factors — it is not a universal constant. We test a small pre-registered set, so we derive our own bar with Holm-Bonferroni rather than importing theirs. Borrowing their number wholesale would be taking an answer instead of doing the calculation.
Method source for: multiple-testing discipline across The signal test
[39] The replication dispute in finance — BOTH SIDESsearch-verified · active academic dispute
Whether the "factor zoo" represents a genuine replication crisis is contested, and we present both sides rather than the one that suits our framing. Critical view: Hou, Xue & Zhang, "Replicating Anomalies" (2020) find many published anomalies weaken badly under more careful implementation; Harvey/Liu/Zhu (above) argue most findings are false; McLean & Pontiff (2016) document decay after publication. Defending view: Chen & Zimmermann (2022) reproduce nearly 100% of published predictors and released open-source code and data to prove it; Jensen, Kelly & Pedersen, "Is There a Replication Crisis in Finance?" (Journal of Finance, 2023, 78(5)) conclude that neither criticism holds — most factors replicate, survive joint modelling, and hold up out-of-sample, and that factors should be seen as a small number of correlated themes rather than hundreds of separate discoveries. Why this matters to us: the "correlated themes" finding is the same lesson our own audit learned the hard way — our four "independent" luck checks turned out to be four views of one question.
Cited on: What we've learned · methodology for The signal test
[40] White — "A Reality Check for Data Snooping" (Econometrica, 2000, 68(5):1097–1126) & Hansen — "A Test for Superior Predictive Ability" (JBES, 2005, 23(4):365–380)search-verified
The two standard tests for the specific question "I tried many strategies and picked the best one — is the winner actually good, or just the luckiest of many?" Hansen's SPA test improves the power of White's original. Not yet implemented in our engine — a known gap, and the correct next step beyond the Holm correction we currently use.
Not yet implemented — logged as an open method gap
[41] Sullivan, Timmermann & White (1999) — data-snooping in technical trading rulessearch-verified
Applies the Reality Check to the exact subject of our signal test: technical trading rules. Tests a large universe of rules and asks whether the best performer survives once you account for how many were tried. The closest published precedent for what this app does.
Method source for: The signal test
[42] Aronson — "Evidence-Based Technical Analysis" (Wiley, 2006)search-verified
Applies the scientific method and statistical inference to trading signals for a general audience — permutation tests, data-snooping bias, and why most published technical analysis fails when tested properly. Older than our ten-year window but still the most direct book-length precedent for this app's whole premise, so it is listed rather than omitted.
Method source for: The signal test · Wealth bookshelf
[43] Newey & West (1987, Econometrica 55(3):703–708) and Newey & West (1994, Review of Economic Studies 61(4):631–653)search-verified
The correction our engine uses when nearby measurements are not independent of each other — the standard fix in econometrics for overlapping windows and autocorrelation. The 1987 paper gives the estimator; the 1994 paper gives automatic lag selection. We currently use a fixed lag equal to the outcome window, not the 1994 automatic method — disclosed as a simplification, not presented as best practice.
Implemented in: The signal test · the hourly lab · cross-validated against an independent implementation to 1.2e-15
[44] Bergmeir, Hyndman & Koo — "A note on the validity of cross-validation for evaluating autoregressive time series prediction" (Computational Statistics & Data Analysis, 2018, 120:70–83)search-verified
Examines when ordinary cross-validation is and is not safe on time-series data — the technical heart of why testing a trading rule is harder than testing a normal statistical model. Time has an order, and most standard statistical tooling quietly assumes it does not.
Method source for: the calibration lab's time-series handling
[45] Block and stationary bootstrap methods — Künsch (1989), Politis & Romano (1994), Shao ("The dependent wild bootstrap")search-verified
The family of resampling methods designed for data where nearby points are related. We tested three of these against our rotation method on our own worst case and reported the result even though two of the alternatives came from this literature and lost: on clustered fires with overlapping windows, block and stationary bootstraps were far too conservative (0.29% where 5% was correct) because they force contiguous runs, over-clustering relative to the real firing pattern.
Tested against in: the calibration lab's null-model shootout
[46] Efron & Tibshirani — "An Introduction to the Bootstrap" (1993) and Phillip Good — "Permutation, Parametric and Bootstrap Tests of Hypotheses"search-verified
The two canonical texts on resampling and permutation testing — the technique behind our shuffle test, our dart control and our bootstrap confidence intervals. Older than the ten-year window but foundational; nothing has replaced them.
Method source for: every randomization test in the app
[47] Talts, Betancourt, Simpson, Vehtari & Gelman — "Validating Bayesian Inference Algorithms with Simulation-Based Calibration" (arXiv:1804.06788, 2018)search-verified
The modern reference for the question "how do you check that your checking-method is itself correct?" The principle generalizes beyond its Bayesian setting: simulate data where you already know the true answer, run your method on it many times, and confirm the answers come out in the right proportions. That principle is exactly what our calibration lab does — feed the engine markets with no edge and confirm it says so.
Method source for: the calibration lab
[48] Cook, Gelman & Rubin — "Validation of software for Bayesian models using posterior quantiles" (Journal of Computational and Graphical Statistics, 2006)search-verified
The earlier paper the method above extends. Establishes the general discipline of validating statistical software, not just statistical theory — the distinction between "the maths is right" and "the code does the maths." Our S22 audit found bugs of exactly the second kind, including a broken random number generator that undermined every randomization test in the app.
Method source for: the calibration lab's invariant tests
[49] Differential testing against independently-audited software (SciPy)live-verified
The most practical check available to a reader who cannot audit our code: run the same numbers through software that neither of us wrote. Our t-distribution, normal distribution, Wilson intervals, ordinary least squares and Newey-West standard errors were all cross-checked against SciPy 1.17 on identical fixed data, agreeing to between 1e-9 and 1e-15. Honest note on how this can mislead: our first Newey-West cross-check passed while barely exercising the correction (the corrected and uncorrected answers differed by 1.00×, meaning the test proved almost nothing). Re-run on clustered data where the correction actually bites, it moved the t-statistic from −3.54 to −1.37 — a verdict-flipping difference — and still matched to 1.2e-15. A passing test on a case that does not stress the code is not evidence.
Applied to: The signal test · the hourly lab
[50] Metamorphic and property-based testingtraining-recall — cross-checkable
The technique for testing a program when you cannot verify its output directly but you do know relationships that must always hold — for example, "an impossibly good result must produce a small p-value" or "the same input must always give the same output." Introduced for programs with no obvious right answer (Chen and colleagues) and now standard in software quality practice, including tools like QuickCheck and Hypothesis. Our calibration lab checks six such properties against many randomly generated datasets rather than a handful of hand-picked examples.
Implemented in: the calibration lab's invariant suite

What we do NOT yet do, stated plainly: we have no out-of-sample holdout, so by our own constitution's gate rules the signal test is "measured, not predictive." We do not implement White's Reality Check, Hansen's SPA test, or the Probability of Backtest Overfitting [37][40]. We use a fixed Newey-West lag rather than automatic selection [43]. These are named here rather than left for a reader to discover, because a methods page that lists only what we do is an advertisement, not a citation.

What this page is not: a promise that every sentence in the app traces to a numbered source — plain arithmetic, mechanism explanations ("how a bond works"), and the app's own live-measured readings (the board, the pack, the tests) don't need external citation because they ARE the primary source, shown working. This registry covers the claims that borrow from the outside world: named studies, regulators, historical events, and licensed datasets.

🪞 The mirror — our self-check ▾ tap to open
What "the mirror" means (it ends every room): after testing everyone else's claims, we turn the same test on ourselves — in two words: self-check. Three questions, every time: what did this page actually prove, what can it NOT know, and did it ever slip from showing you what IS into telling you what to DO? A page anywhere else that never holds a mirror to itself is telling you something too.
The mirror, for a page that cites sources: a citation isn't proof by itself — it's a pointer you can go check. This registry can be wrong (a source can be misread, a link can rot, a study can be superseded), and the honest fix isn't to trust it more, it's to click through. The tier tags exist so you know exactly how hard we checked each one — and training-recall entries are flagged as the weakest tier on purpose. If a citation here ever looks thin, that's worth telling us.
