How to read a company

A stock is a slice of a real business, and four times a year US law forces every public company to show its homework — the quarterly report. Almost nobody reads it; the few who do have an honest edge over the many who trade the headline. This page teaches the report in plain words, shows you which numbers matter and which ones lie, and gives you a live tool: type any ticker and see what the company's own insiders have been filing. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

📄 The report — one story told as a ladder of numbers

Every income statement is the same story: money comes in the top, costs eat it on the way down, and what survives at the bottom belongs to the owners. Read it as a ladder — and know that the higher up the ladder, the harder the number is to fake; the lower down, the more room for makeup.

Revenue — the top
All the money customers paid. The hardest number to dress up. First question: is it growing, and is it growing because more customers or just higher prices?
EBIT — the engine number
Profit from actually running the business — revenue minus the costs of making and selling the product — before interest payments and taxes. Why strip those out? Interest depends on how much the company borrowed and taxes depend on where it lives; EBIT shows whether the machine itself makes money, comparable across companies.
EBITDA — handle with tongs
EBIT with depreciation added back — pretending the wear-and-tear on factories, trucks, and equipment costs nothing. Sometimes a fair lens (software firms with few machines); often a flattering one (for an airline, planes wearing out is the WHOLE cost). Buffett's famous jab: does management think the tooth fairy pays for the equipment?
Net income — the official bottom line
What's left after everything, including interest and taxes. The number headlines quote — and the one with the most accounting makeup on it, because one-time items and estimates all land here.
EPS — the per-slice number
Net income divided by the number of shares. Watch the share count: a company can grow EPS by buying back shares while the actual business shrinks — or quietly cut YOUR slice by printing new shares (dilution).
Free cash flow — the truth serum
Actual cash generated minus what was spent keeping the business alive. Profits are an opinion (built on estimates); cash is a fact (either it's in the bank or it isn't). When reported profit and real cash tell different stories for quarters on end, believe the cash.

What truly matters — the four questions the whole report answers:

1. Is the business growing? (revenue, over years — not one quarter)
2. Does it actually make money doing what it does? (EBIT and its margin, trend over time)
3. Does profit turn into real cash? (free cash flow vs. net income — the honesty check)
4. Who is the profit for — and could debt take it first? (share count trend; total debt vs. cash. A great business drowning in debt is the bank's business, not yours.)

Everything else — the adjusted numbers, the excitement, the guidance — is commentary on those four.

Red flags, in plain words: "adjusted" profit growing while real cash shrinks, quarter after quarter · money owed BY customers growing much faster than sales (booking sales that haven't really been collected) · a new "one-time cost" every single quarter (then it's not one-time — it's the business) · share count quietly rising every year (your slice thinning) · the auditor quitting or the finance chief leaving suddenly. None of these is proof of anything — each is a go-look-closer, which is exactly what the needle's worst grade means too.

Where to get all of it, free: every report lives on SEC EDGAR — the US government's public library of company filings. The quarterly report is called a 10-Q, the big annual one a 10-K. No account, no fee. The same source powers the tool below.

🕵 Insiders — what the people running the company do with their own shares

The law: executives, board members, and 10%+ owners must publicly report every trade in their own company's stock within two business days, on a filing called a Form 4. So the buying and selling of the people with the best possible information is a matter of public record — most people just never look.

Why it matters — and the famous asymmetry: insiders sell for a hundred reasons (a house, taxes, divorce, diversifying, a scheduled plan) — so a sale usually means little. But there is essentially one reason an insider spends their own cash buying more of a stock they're already overexposed to: they think it's going higher. That's why researchers and old hands watch open-market buys — especially cluster buys (several insiders buying around the same time) — far more than sales.

Honest caveats: many insider trades run on pre-set autopilot plans and say nothing about today's view · insiders are often early by years, and they're wrong plenty · and reading these filings is 100% legal — the illegal thing (what "insider trading" means in the news) is trading on secret information, which is exactly what the two-day public filing rule exists to police. You reading Form 4s is the system working as designed.

🗣 The verified voices — do fundamentals still matter, or do some markets just play movement?

The honest frame comes from Graham, and everything since has confirmed it:

In the short run the market is a voting machine — counting popularity, mood, and momentum. In the long run it is a weighing machine — weighing the actual earnings and cash of the business.
Benjamin Graham's famous distinction (paraphrased), the spine of The Intelligent Investor — and of this whole page.
Buffett's version: price is what you pay, value is what you get — and his repeated warning that only when the tide goes out do you learn who's been swimming naked. Sound financials are the swimsuit: invisible in a boom, decisive in a bust. His verified record was built on the weighing machine, held for decades.
Warren Buffett — shareholder letters, public record; the top shelf of our bookshelf.
Bogle's twist: if reading companies is hard and most pros fail at it (SPIVA: ~92% of US stock funds[5] lost to their benchmarks over the 20 years through 2025), owning ALL the companies cheaply means the weighing machine works FOR you without you ever reading a single 10-Q. Buy-and-hold indexing is fundamentals investing with the stock-picking removed.
John Bogle — and the scorekeeper data that keeps proving him right.

So does any of this apply to speculation — or do some markets "just play movement"? The honest split:

Where the weighing machine exists (stocks, bonds): there IS a scale under the price — earnings, cash, interest payments. Prices can detach from it for years (Japan did for a decade going up and three decades coming back), but the scale is real, and long-run returns have tracked it. This is why buy-and-hold works where it works: you're not betting on the vote, you're waiting for the weigh-in.

Where there is no scale (most of crypto, memecoins, short-term currency bets): no earnings, no cash flow, no 10-Q — there is nothing to weigh, so ALL there is is the voting machine. Those markets really are pure movement: positioning, crowd, story, flow. That's not a slur — it's a structural fact, and it's exactly why our crypto tools read positioning (funding, crowding, liquidations, who's leaning where) instead of fundamentals: we built the instrument the market actually supports.

Can playing movement work? Momentum is a genuinely documented pattern in academic data — the voting machine has measurable habits. The same evidence base says the profits are mostly eaten by costs and crowding, and the people who try it retail overwhelmingly lose (Barber–Odean: ~80% of day-traders lose; under 1% reliably profit after fees). Schwager's verified winners who DID play movement all shared one trait — obsession with position sizing and not dying — and Thorp, the one scorekeeper-verified edge-player on our shelf, tells nearly everyone not to try. So: movement-playing exists, is real, and is a professional's game with a documented record of amateurs — while the weighing machine is the only mechanism in markets that works without you being faster or smarter than the crowd.

One sentence to keep: read the financials where financials exist; read the crowd where they don't; and never confuse which market you're standing in.

🧺 Indexing — and the question it raises: is Buffett telling you to buy blind?

What an index is: just a list. The S&P 500 is a list of the ~500 biggest US companies, each counted by its size — bigger company, bigger slice of the list. An index fund is a fund that simply owns the whole list and does nothing else: no picking, no predicting, no trading around. You own a sliver of every business on it.

The word "cheap" — the single most misunderstood word in investing: when Bogle and Buffett say buy the index cheaply, they do NOT mean "buy stocks that are cheap." They mean the fee — what the fund charges you per year (the expense ratio). An index fund can charge 0.03% because there's nobody to pay for guessing. Watch what that difference alone does: $10,000 growing 40 years at market returns keeps about $148,000 in a 0.03% fund and about $103,000 in a 1% fund — the fee quietly ate 31% of the final wealth, with identical investments underneath. That arithmetic — not stock-picking genius — is most of why indexing beats the pros.

So is it "blindly buying everything"? Three honest answers — no, no, and one real yes:

No — it's a specific, deliberate claim, not blindness: businesses as a whole earn profits over time, and I'd rather own the whole earning machine than bet I can spot its best parts. That's the weighing machine again — you're just weighing ALL of it at once instead of piece by piece.

No — the list cleans itself. A company that collapses shrinks and drops off the index automatically; a rising one grows into it — with no prediction from you. For once, survivorship bias works FOR the holder: the index is always, by construction, whatever survived. You never had to read a single report to shed the failures.

And the one real YES — the paradox worth knowing: an index buys at whatever the market price is, trusting that price is roughly sane. But prices are only sane because somebody else did read the financials — the active minority doing the 10-Q homework this page teaches sets the prices the index accepts. Indexing free-rides on the readers. Economists even have a name for the endpoint (if literally everyone indexed, nobody would set prices and picking would pay again); Bogle's answer, which the data still supports, was that we're nowhere near that point — trillions still chase active picking, and SPIVA shows most of it still loses. So the free ride remains genuinely free for now, and the page you're reading exists partly so the reading minority stays informed.

Buffett's actual position — not what the headline version says: Buffett has read financial reports nearly every day for seventy years; his fortune IS the weighing machine, applied personally, with obsessive company-reading. He is not against reading — he's against pretending. His advice splits people honestly in two: if you will genuinely do the work — read the filings, know the businesses, stay rational when prices fall — then knowing what you own is his whole method. If you won't or can't make it your job (his blunt term is the "know-nothing investor," meaning no shame, just honesty about your hours), then the index gets you the weighing machine's result without the reading — and his public $1M bet proved even the professionals mostly fail to beat it. His own estate instructions: 90% into a low-cost S&P 500 index fund. So the answer to the question is: he advocates knowing which of the two you are. The blind move isn't indexing — the blind move is stock-picking without reading, which is most of what actually happens.

The honest catches indexing carries (it's un-picked, not un-risky): the index concentrates in whatever is currently biggest — size-weighting means it automatically holds the most of whatever has already risen most, so today that's a handful of giant tech companies; the whole market can still fall by half and stay down for years (the Japan card in our record is the extreme case); and an index of a casino is still a casino — indexing works where a weighing machine exists under the prices, which is the stocks-and-bonds side of the split above, not the pure-movement markets.

💳 The paid tools — what they actually sell (a reference map, not a shopping list)

Yes, an entire industry digests this data for paying customers. Three tiers, so you know the landscape when you meet it:

Institutional terminals — Bloomberg (roughly $25–30k per seat per year), FactSet, S&P Capital IQ: every filing, transcript, and estimate, one keystroke away. The AI layer — services like AlphaSense run language models across millions of filings and earnings calls, answering questions like "who mentioned margin pressure this quarter" in seconds. Quant funds have machine-read filings for decades — counting cautious words in 10-Ks, flagging tone shifts on calls, tracking insider patterns. And retail subscriptions sell smaller versions of all of it: insider-trade trackers, AI stock scores, screeners with confidence numbers attached.

Notice what powers nearly all of it: the same free government data this page's own checker reads. They sell speed, breadth, and packaging on top of a public library. And an honest scale check on ourselves: this project is not a free version of those tools and doesn't pretend to be — they industrialize the whole library (every company, every filing, parsed and cross-referenced in seconds); our checker opens the door to one shelf at a time. Xplainsight is a free teaching instrument: it shows you the library exists, teaches you to read what's on the shelf, and proves the door was never locked. The paid tools are for people whose job is reading the whole library daily. Different things — and only one of them can be free.

Is it reliable? Split the product in two and the question answers itself:

The data layer (the sensor) — genuinely reliable. These firms accurately organize what the filings say; a terminal rarely misquotes a balance sheet. If the product is "here's the record, fast," it delivers, and the price buys time, not truth.

The judgment layer (the interpreter) — where confidence outruns evidence. The moment the product turns data into a score, ranking, or "AI signal," it becomes testable against the scorekeepers — and the verdict exists: professional funds wielding these exact tools, with more computing power and data than any subscription will ever give you, still lost to their benchmarks about 92% of the time over the 20 years through 2025 (SPIVA). Academic studies of machine-read filings find the patterns are real but the profits are small and decaying — once a pattern is published, the crowd trades it away. (Both-sides honesty on our own favorite scorekeeper: an industry-sponsored 2026 study argues SPIVA's method — counting every closed fund as a loser and weighting tiny funds equally — overstates the failure rate, putting dollar-weighted odds nearer a coin flip; SPIVA and independent reviewers defend the method. The dispute is real; even the critics' friendliest number still means no reliable way to pick the winners in advance.)

The test to carry (same one as the bookshelf's bottom shelf): a genuinely reliable signal wouldn't be for sale — a firm holding a durable edge harvests it quietly, the way the verified winners did, until imitators crowd it out. The economics of selling a signal work best when the product's real value is the customer's belief. So before paying for any judgment layer, ask where the seller's money actually comes from: using the method, or selling it?

The AI-era warning, specifically: language models make interpretation cheaper, faster, and — the dangerous part — more fluent. An AI-written stock thesis reads more confident than a human one while resting on the same unresolved uncertainty. Fluency is not accuracy; this era mass-produces confident wrongness in beautiful prose, which is precisely the thing an evidence grade exists to catch.

The fair closing word: these services aren't scams — the data layer is real, and their analysts are part of the "reading minority" that keeps prices sane for everyone (including every indexer). They're reliable as a library, unreliable as an oracle — and the subscription price is mostly for the oracle part. We name these companies as landscape, not endorsement: this project recommends no product, and everything its own tools read is free.

The timescale and the opponent — a clarifier, honestly tiered.

On the record (his own published words and actions): Buffett reads companies over years, not quarters — he has publicly criticized the market's quarterly-guidance obsession, and his letters describe judging a business's long-run earning power from its filings. The "know-something vs. know-nothing investor" split is his own published language. The 90%-index estate instruction is in his 2013 shareholder letter. Mr. Market — the moody neighbor shouting a new price every day, whom you ignore until his mood makes the price absurd — is Graham's published teaching, which Buffett has endorsed for decades.

Our reading of it (interpretation — marked as such, because we never spoke to the man): stitching those pieces together, we read his method as: weigh businesses over years using their filings, act only when the market's mood misprices them against that weight — and tell everyone unwilling to do that weighing to own the whole scale cheaply instead. In this reading, the quarterly report is where you verify the machine is still the machine; the belief is built on the long record. And the know-nothing investor is not his opponent but his audience — his opponent is the market's mood itself, and indexing is how he tells the audience to put that same mood to work for them (buying through every swing automatically) instead of against them.

That second paragraph is a synthesis, not a quote — reasoned from the record, unconfirmed by its subject, and open to challenge like every interpretation on this project. If his published record and our reading ever conflict, the record wins.

🧾 The checkout screen — what actually happens when you press Buy

Two prices, always. Every stock has a buying price and a selling price, a hair apart — the gap (the "spread") is the toll booth you pass through both ways. Tiny on big famous stocks; wide on small obscure ones. You pay it invisibly, on every single trade, which is one more reason trading LESS is the cheapest strategy on this site.

Two ways to order. A market order says "buy NOW at whatever the price is" — fast, but in a fast market "whatever" can surprise you (that surprise is called slippage). A limit order says "buy only at this price or better" — you control the price, but might not get filled. Neither is wrong; knowing which you're sending is the whole lesson.

"Free" trading has a checkout too. Many zero-commission apps are paid by routing your orders to trading firms (payment for order flow) — legal, disclosed in fine print, and worth knowing because it explains HOW free is free: you're not paying a fee; you're the order being sold.
