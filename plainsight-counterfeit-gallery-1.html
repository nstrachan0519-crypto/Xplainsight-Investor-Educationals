🎭 The counterfeit gallery

Most stolen money isn't taken by hackers — it's handed over, convinced. The costumes change every year (coins, gurus, "AI trading bots," romance), but underneath, nearly every money scam is one of a few machines, and every machine fails the same five tells. Learn five things, spot a thousand. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

The five tells — each one alone is enough to walk away

Tell 1 — Guaranteed returns. "12% a month, guaranteed." Real markets cannot guarantee returns; even government bonds only promise their stated interest if held to the end. The guarantee IS the confession. Our Who Called It room shows even the world's most famous experts couldn't call outcomes — someone promising certainty is selling the impossible.
Tell 2 — The clock. "Only 3 spots left." "Price doubles Friday." Real investments do not expire like coupons. Urgency exists for exactly one reason: to stop you from checking — because checking kills it (Tell 5).
Tell 3 — Secrecy. "A loophole THEY don't want known." "Don't tell your bank why you're withdrawing." Real opportunities survive being described to a skeptical friend. Any pitch that needs quiet is telling you what daylight would do to it. (Banks specifically train tellers to ask — the scammer coaching you past that question is the whole story.)
Tell 4 — They hold the money. Send it to THEIR wallet, THEIR platform, THEIR "account manager." One structural question defeats this forever: who is the custodian? Legitimate finance keeps your money at an independent company in YOUR name — Madoff's entire fraud lived in being his own custodian, and it's the same rule our Getting Help room applies to real advisors.
Tell 5 — The track record can't be checked. Screenshots, testimonials, "verified by our members." Real advisers appear in free government databases (adviserinfo.sec.gov, brokercheck.finra.org); real funds have audited, public records. If the proof is only what THEY show you, it isn't proof — it's a brochure.

The species — same machines, different costumes

The Ponzi — paying Paul with Peter's deposit
No investing happens. Early "returns" are just newer victims' money passed backward, which makes the returns look smooth and reliable — the exact bait. It dies the day withdrawals exceed deposits, taking everyone still inside. Fails tells 1, 4, and 5 at once. The living museum's FTX case shows the modern costume: real company, real app, same hole where the money should be.
The pump and dump — the crowd IS the exit
Insiders load up on something cheap and thin, manufacture excitement ("the next big coin!"), and sell into the crowd they summoned. The buyers aren't the customers — they're the product being sold to. Fails tells 2 and 3. Our positioning gauges exist partly so you can see when you're being invited to be someone's exit.
Affinity fraud — trust, borrowed then spent
Runs through churches, immigrant communities, clubs, friend groups — because trust inside a community skips the checking. The pitch arrives pre-verified by a face you know (who is usually also a victim). The defense is uncomfortable but simple: the five tells apply to people you love. Verifying isn't an insult; it's what protects them too.
The long-con romance ("pig-butchering") — the largest-dollar scam running
Weeks or months of friendship or romance first — no money mentioned. Then a casual "I've been doing well with this platform..." The platform is fake, shows fake gains, even lets you withdraw a little (bait), then takes everything on the big deposit. Regulators worldwide report this species now steals more than any other, in the billions yearly. Fails tells 3, 4, and 5 — and adds the cruelest one: the relationship WAS the scam's product. Anyone you've never met in person who steers talk to investing is the pattern, full stop.
The guru — the course is the yacht
Sells the secret of trading riches for $997. The honest question from our bookshelf: if the method mints money, why is selling courses the business? Their verified income stream is your card, not the market. Fails tell 5 (unaudited screenshots) and usually tell 2 (enrollment "closes Friday"). Note the overlap with the finfluencer economics in our behavior room — this is that machine's paid tier.
The recovery scam — robbing the wounded twice
After any loss, "agents" appear offering to recover your money — for an upfront fee. They found you because victim lists are sold. No legitimate recovery service charges upfront and messages you first. The one aimed at people this gallery just failed to save; it's here so it can't get them twice.
Tell 4, turned into architecture: the trustworthy configuration is always the one that can't take your money, not the one that promises it won't. Any site, app, person, or "opportunity" that fails that test has told you everything. (This app holds itself to the same test — see how to spot fake builds of Xplainsight on the home page.)

If it already happened: no shame — these machines are built by professionals and beat smart people daily (several exhibits in our living museum were run BY professionals). Move fast and boring: stop all contact, contact your bank/card immediately, report it (in the US: reportfraud.ftc.gov and ic3.gov), and treat anyone who contacts you about recovery as the sixth species above.

🪞 The mirror — our self-check ▾ tap to open
What "the mirror" means (it ends every room): after testing everyone else's claims, we turn the same test on ourselves — in two words: self-check. Three questions, every time: what did this page actually prove, what can it NOT know, and did it ever slip from showing you what IS into telling you what to DO? A page anywhere else that never holds a mirror to itself is telling you something too.
The mirror, as always: this room accused no one and named no living villains — it showed you machines, because machines repeat and costumes don't. Five tells, checkable in minutes, mostly on free government websites. The counterfeits require your hurry and your trust-without-checking; withholding those two things costs nothing and defeats nearly all of them. What you do with any opportunity remains yours — this room just makes sure it's really you deciding.
