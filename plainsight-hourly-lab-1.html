The hourly lab

The same signals from the signal test — moving-average crosses, RSI, MACD, buy-the-dip — re-run on real hourly Bitcoin and Ethereum prices, about a year's worth, fetched live from a free public source. Plus two things only hourly data can show: how zooming in makes noise look like a pattern, and what trading more often actually costs you. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

Why hourly, and why only now: the main signal test uses data back to 1871 — great for long history, but it only checks prices once a month. That's too slow to show what fast traders actually do.

Free data lets us look back about one year, checked every single hour, for Bitcoin and Ethereum. We picked these two because people trade them all day and all night — so an "hourly price" is a real trade, not a stale guess.

One year is a lot less than 150 years, and we say that plainly. Because this dataset is short and fast, we add two extra checks below (not fewer) — a short, fast dataset needs MORE defense against fooling itself, not less.

📡 How the data is fetched (and why it's allowed) ▾ tap to open

CoinGecko is the company we get crypto prices from. Their free plan gives an hourly price automatically — but only for requests asking about 90 days or less at a time.

To get a full year, we ask 4 separate times per coin (about 89 days each) and glue the answers together. That's 8 requests total — the free plan allows up to 10,000 requests a month, so this barely touches it. CoinGecko's own instructions recommend doing exactly this to build a one-year hourly chart. We are not sneaking around a rule. We are using the method they wrote for us.

What we will NOT do: get prices every 5 minutes for anything more than one day back. That level of detail only comes with a paid plan, and this app promises to stay free forever. Some people try to trick the system — asking for 5-minute prices one day at a time, over and over, until they've rebuilt a whole year. CoinGecko's rules say you can't even try that, whether or not it would work. So we say no for two separate reasons: it breaks the rules, and — even if it didn't — it wouldn't actually hand back real 5-minute prices from the past on this plan anyway.

We check, we don't just assume: every time this page grabs new prices, it measures the real time gap between them and tells you plainly whether it actually got an hourly price. If CoinGecko ever sends something less detailed, you'll see that stated here — not a label that quietly says "hourly" when it isn't.

🔍 The zoom-in illusion

Same coin, same stretch of time — two speeds. Zoom in and every wiggle looks meaningful; zoom out and it's a smooth line. Nothing about the market changed between the two charts below — only how often we looked. That's the trap behind a lot of "I see a pattern": finer data doesn't mean more truth, it means more noise dressed up as detail.

💸 The overtrading tax

We take one real rule — a fast/slow moving-average cross — and actually trade it two ways on the same data: checked every hour, and checked once a day. Same rule, same coin, same stretch of time. Every time the rule flips, that's a trade, and every trade costs a fee. Check more often, and you get more flips, which means more fees — whether or not the rule is actually any good.

🆕 The Newey-West correction — verify it before trusting it ▾ tap to open

Newey-West (say it: NEW-ee WEST) is a math fix named after two economists. Here's the problem it fixes: our test looks at the 24 hours AFTER a signal fires. If two signals fire only 5 hours apart, their 24-hour windows overlap — they share most of the same hours. They're not really two separate stories. Counting them as fully separate anyway can trick us into feeling more sure than we should.

The main signal test's own notes have said "Newey-West: planned, not built yet" since session 19. It gets built here first, because hourly data has the worst overlap problem in the whole app.

We didn't just trust the formula and ship it. We tested it on a fake dataset first — one where we already KNOW the true answer. The fake data is pure random noise (a random walk), so there is truly no real pattern in it, by design. A trustworthy test should still wrongly shout "I found something!" about 5 times out of 100 tries — no more, no less.

Here's what actually happened when we ran it: the OLD, uncorrected way of testing shouted "found something" LESS than 5 times out of 100 — not more, like we first guessed it would. That's still wrong, just wrong in the other direction: being too cautious is a mistake too, not only being too confident. We wrote that down instead of hiding it.

What matters is this: does the Newey-West version land closer to the fair 5-in-100 target than the old way did? Press the button and see the real numbers below — reported honestly, whichever way they point.

📐 Full method & every parameter

Data: CoinGecko's free plan, the market_chart/range address, BTC and ETH only, 4 chunks of 89 days per coin, about 356 days total. We check every run to confirm it's really hourly (never assumed). Price only — no trading volume, no order-book depth.

Signals (edge-triggered — a signal only counts the first hour a rule becomes true, checked line-by-line against look-ahead): a 10-hour/50-hour moving-average cross; 24-hour momentum turning positive; buy-the-dip at −5% from the 24-hour high and −10% from the 168-hour (7-day) high — smaller than the daily test's −10%/−20% because hourly price swings are smaller too, said openly, not hidden; hourly RSI-14 crossing 70 or 30; MACD(12,26,9) on hourly bars, using the same numbers real traders reuse at every timeframe.

Outcome: what happened to price over the next 24 hours after each signal — decided in advance, before any test ran.

Controls (things we compare every signal against): what happened across all hours no matter what (the baseline); a blind-dart pick of random hours, run 3,000 times (the dart test); and four rules built to mean nothing on purpose, which should score around average (the nonsense check) — every 17th hour, an hour-of-day that's a prime number, an odd-numbered hour, or a price ending in the digit 7.

Checking for luck, two separate ways: a shuffle test (4,000 reshuffles, no assumptions about the shape of the data) and the new Newey-West test, which is honest about overlapping hours (uses a Bartlett kernel, a weighting rule; self-tested above before we trusted it). Testing many signals at once: a correction called Benjamini-Hochberg, allowing up to a 10-in-100 fluke rate — looser than the main test's usual bar, since this is a first pass on brand-new data, said openly.

Staying stable over time, adapted for one short year: the main test checks whether a signal holds up across several real economic regimes (eras) — but one year doesn't contain more than one. So here we use monthly-block stability instead: 12 chunks of 30 days, checking that the sign agrees in at least two out of every three chunks with enough fires to judge (minimum 3 fires per chunk, minimum 3 qualifying chunks). We also check non-overlapping fires — cases spaced at least 24 hours apart, so each one is a genuinely separate story.

Economic significance: you can set your own cost-per-trade above (0.20% by default) — an edge smaller than that isn't worth trading even if the math says it's real.

What this page cannot tell you (we say this about ourselves too): one year of data is close to being just one market regime — we can't claim a signal holds up across both rising and falling markets the way the 150-year daily test can, and we say so instead of hiding it. No costs beyond the one flat fee are built in — real trading always costs a bit more than our model shows. The Newey-West self-test checks that the FORMULA works, not that any one signal is actually worth trading. And this whole page has not yet been checked and approved by the project's own review process.
