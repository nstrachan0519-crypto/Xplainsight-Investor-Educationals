Our findings

This app has now run real tests on real data — and testing taught us things no single verdict could. This room is the ledger: what our own machines established, what remains genuinely unknown, and what today's traders believe that we haven't tested yet. Everything here was earned, not assumed. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

What our own tests established

lesson ▾ tap to open
1 · Moves tend to continue — that much is real. Across 150 years of monthly data, trend-style signals kept showing persistence: a market that's been rising is slightly more likely to keep rising than chance allows. The technical name is autocorrelation (the academic literature calls the tradeable version time-series momentum). This survived our hardest checks — the nonsense floor, the random-entry dart, reshuffling the data thousands of times to see if the pattern was just luck, and checking it still holds up decade by decade, not just in one lucky stretch.
lesson ▾ tap to open
2 · WHY moves continue is genuinely unsettled — and pretending otherwise would be a lie. At least five explanations compete: slow reaction to news, herding, the disposition effect, payment-for-risk (no psychology at all), and slow-moving institutional money. After 30+ years, academia has not crowned a winner — momentum is famously the best-documented finding whose cause remains disputed. Our behavior room says "herding"; the precise truth is "herding is one suspect among five."
3 · The amount of data and the amount of evidence are different things. 150 years of overlapping 12-month windows is really only ~150 independent stories — and one dramatic year retold monthly is still one year. This lesson (caught by this app's own builder) now lives inside the Signal Test as two fixes: counting each stretch of time only once instead of double-dipping overlapping windows, and adjusting the math when signals share the same underlying days. It applies far beyond markets: ten headlines repeating one source is one source.
4 · A verdict is only as honest as its control. Our first control was accidentally biased (it compared buy-signals against mostly-bad months — a tailwind). The fix: a random-entry "dart" control and a nonsense-signal floor — four deliberately meaningless rules that must score at chance, or the whole test is exposed. They scored exactly at chance. Any signal that can't beat our junk rules has found nothing.
complete record shown ▾ tap to open
5 · Rare events can't be graded like frequent ones. A signal that fires ten times, all clustered around two generational bubbles, can never earn a real score — not because it's false, but because scoring needs lots of repeats to trust, and ten rare events spread over a century isn't lots. Forcing a normal pass/fail grade onto it would fake a confident answer nobody's entitled to. Our fix: rare-event signals get their complete record shown instead of a fake verdict. A generational warning is a short story, never a statistic.
6 · Famous signals are a survivor's bracket. We test signals that became famous — and they became famous partly because they once seemed to work. The forgotten failures never made our list. That contamination can't be fully fixed, only disclosed — and it's disclosed on the Signal Test's methodology card, along with the deeper version: the US market itself is history's surviving market.
7 · "150 years" is really four or five different markets. Pre-Fed gold standard, interwar, Bretton Woods, post-1971 fiat, modern electronic — different money, different index (the S&P wasn't 500 stocks until 1957), different laws and players. A signal that worked in only one era is probably an artifact of that era. This is why our tests check sign-stability across three eras rather than trusting one long average.
lesson ▾ tap to open
9 · Outside review makes the instrument sharper — and verification catches what review can't. An external AI review of our method scored it well and suggested upgrades; per our rules the praise earned nothing, but the suggestions were adopted on merit: evidence-language verdicts ("strong/moderate/mixed/little/insufficient historical evidence" — because "appears true" claimed more than history can), effect sizes, bootstrap intervals, an is-it-economically-meaningful line, regime breakdowns, and parameter robustness (if only RSI-14 works but 10/12/16/18 don't, the famous setting was luck). And in wiring those upgrades, our own pre-ship verification caught a real unit bug that would have inflated one number 21-fold — the reviewer didn't catch it; the known-answer test did. Review improves the design; only verification protects the numbers.
8 · Markets structurally can't contain some things people sell. Established by reasoning, no data needed: 24/7 markets cannot have gap patterns (no close, no gap), and spot currency volume does not exist anywhere at any price (no central exchange). Tools claiming to read numbers nature doesn't produce have completed their own review.

The big open question

What don't we know? Why persistence exists (five suspects, no conviction) · when herding flips to anti-herding — newer research finds crowds moving together after some shocks but scattering after others, so the crowd isn't even reliably a crowd · whether algorithms and AI amplify or dampen all of this (brand-new research territory — including studies of whether AI models themselves herd) · and whether any measured edge survives its own fame, since a pattern widely believed gets traded away or distorted by the believing (reflexivity). Anyone claiming certainty on these four is ahead of the world's evidence.

The modern playbook — what today's traders believe (and what's testable)

The old retail world ran on RSI and MACD. The new one — exploding in popularity since roughly 2021 — is called "Smart Money Concepts" (SMC), largely from educator Michael Huddleston ("Inner Circle Trader" / ICT). Its core story: big institutions must hunt small traders' stop-loss orders to fill their own huge positions, so price moves are engineered "liquidity hunts" — and reading those footprints is the edge. It's a modern rebuild of Wyckoff's century-old cycle theory with new vocabulary. We map it here the way we map everything: plainly, and sorted by whether it can be tested.

Liquidity sweeps TESTABLE — ON OUR BENCH
The claim: price deliberately pokes below an obvious recent low (where stop-losses cluster), triggers those stops, then reverses — the "sweep and reverse." Why it's testable: "broke the prior low during the day but closed back above it" is exact math on daily Open-High-Low-Close data — which our bring-your-own-CSV loader already reads. Promoters claim 65–75% win rates on these; one even claims 80% of certain sweeps reverse within minutes, uncited. Numbers that specific are invitations. TESTED (July 2026, daily S&P 2013–26, rules locked in writing before we ran the numbers): the famous BULLISH sweep-and-reverse ("stops hunted, buy the reversal") failed every check — 150 fires, 59% up vs 64% base, worse than doing nothing. The unfashionable BEARISH mirror passed 5 of 6: returns after upside sweeps averaged +0.14% vs the +0.49% norm (permutation p=0.003, era-stable) — a real but modest dampening, not a crash signal (the market still rose 60% of the time). The marketed setup failed; its mirror holds a small true effect nobody sells. Full battery lives in the Signal Test's daily mode.
Fair value gaps (FVGs) TESTABLE — ON OUR BENCH
The claim: when a candle moves so fast that its neighbors' wicks don't overlap it, the skipped zone is "unfinished business" price returns to fill. Why it's testable: the three-candle gap is exact arithmetic, and "did price return to the zone within N bars, more often than chance?" is a countable question. Widely called the most-searched concept in the method. TESTED (July 2026, same data): as a trade signal it failed everything — 66% up vs a 65% nonsense floor, indistinguishable from junk. The famous "gaps fill" claim: 79.7% filled within 20 days — but an arbitrary same-width placebo zone got touched 68.8% of the time, so most celebrated "fills" are just markets wandering. Real excess ≈11 points, modest, and a ceiling (the gap zone sits nearer price than the placebo by construction).
Kill zones & the "Silver Bullet" NEEDS DATA WE DON'T HAVE FREE
The claim: specific clock hours (London open, New York morning) contain the real institutional moves; one famous one-hour setup is sold with "quit your job in 90 days" energy. Why we can't test it: these are time-of-day claims needing intraday data, which isn't freely available at depth (we checked — it's paid-vendor territory, ruled out by our vows). Honest note from inside the movement itself: independent backtesting reportedly puts the Silver Bullet near 50–65%, well below the 70–80% commonly claimed. We can't verify either number — which is exactly the problem with claims that live where free data doesn't.
Order blocks FLEXIBLE AS TAUGHT — BORDERLINE UNFALSIFIABLE
The claim: the last opposite-colored candle before a big move marks where institutions loaded up, and price respects that zone on return. The problem: as commonly taught, which candle "counts," how big the move must be, and what "respecting" means all flex after the fact — the same disease as Elliott waves. A strict, pre-registered version could be tested — so we wrote one and TESTED it (July 2026, same data): the claim failed in both directions. Bullish "support" retests: 44% up vs 64% base, avg −0.56% vs +0.49% — returns were BELOW normal after touching the zone (era-stable, small sample of 25). Bearish "resistance": the market rose MORE than normal after touching it (+1.03% vs +0.49%). The deeper finding: strictly defined, the concept dies; taught loosely, it can't be tested — either way the seller wins and the buyer can't check. The strict version now lives in the Signal Test's daily mode so anyone can re-run it.
Power of Three (accumulation → manipulation → distribution) HINDSIGHT-SHAPED
The claim: every session secretly has three acts — a quiet range, a fake move to trap traders, then the real move. The problem: any day's chart can be carved into three acts after it's over; even sympathetic voices inside the movement concede the hindsight-bias criticism is fair. Without a rule that names the acts in advance, this is a story format, not a prediction — and story formats always fit.

The honest synthesis: SMC's underlying intuition — that large orders leave footprints and stop-clusters attract price — is not crazy; it's related to real market microstructure. But the method as sold mixes two exact, testable claims (sweeps, FVGs) with a vocabulary of flexible, after-the-fact ones — and the win rates in the marketing are precisely the kind of numbers that never arrive with a public test attached. So we'll do what nobody selling it does: pre-register the exact math and run it. Results will land in the Signal Test's daily mode, whichever way they point.

The open test bench

Done: the liquidity-sweep and FVG tests (results above — pre-registered, full battery, detectors known-answer verified first). Done also: the strict order-block test (results above — the definition was writable; the claim failed both directions). Queued: 1) cross-market replication — the engine now accepts any market's CSV unchanged, so every finding above awaits its test on markets it has never seen · 2) from external peer review: Bayes factors and parameter-stability heatmaps. Each will face the same nonsense floor, dart control, and permutation testing as everything else — and each gets reported here, pass or fail. That's the deal this whole app makes: we test what people believe, we show every angle, and the reader keeps the verdict.

Three questions, every time: what did this page actually prove, what can it NOT know, and did it ever slip from showing you what IS into telling you what to DO? A page anywhere else that never holds a mirror to itself is telling you something too.
The mirror, as always: this page is a ledger of what testing taught us — including what it taught us about our own first mistakes (the biased control, the forced verdicts on rare events). Nothing here tells you to trade anything. The most valuable thing on this page isn't any finding; it's the pattern: every belief, including ours, goes on the bench — and the bench is public.
