The living museum of losses

Every market museum celebrates winners. This one keeps the wreckage — the great documented blowups, each replayed through the same lens our live gauges use: what the data showed at the time, what it couldn't show, and what the crowd did anyway. Everything here already happened and already resolved — that's the rule. We teach the herd lesson only in the past tense, because reality has already graded the exam. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

Why a living museum of losses exists: the flat wallets on our live board are today's small wrecks. These are history's great ones — and they rhyme. Watch for the same five ingredients in every exhibit: borrowed size (leverage), a confident story, a crowd piling in, a trigger nobody modeled, and forced selling that feeds itself (the doom loop). The costumes change — bonds, coins, exchanges. The machine doesn't.

Exhibit 1 · 1998 · Bonds — before crypto existed
LTCM — the smartest room in finance, gone in four months

The story going in: a hedge fund staffed by two Nobel Prize winners and Wall Street's most famous bond traders, running mathematical convergence trades. Returns of 20–43% a year, four years running. The market's verdict: geniuses who couldn't lose.

The structure underneath: the trades earned tiny, near-certain gaps — so to make big returns, the fund borrowed enormously: about $30 of positions for every $1 of its own money (28-to-1). At the end of 1997 the partners returned $2.7 billion to investors while keeping positions the same size — voluntarily raising their own leverage right before the storm. Our cushion gauge in one line: their room-to-fall was tiny by construction, and they made it tinier on purpose.

The trigger nobody modeled: August 1998, Russia defaulted on its own debt. Panicked money worldwide fled everything risky at once — and "uncorrelated" positions all fell together, because in a panic everyone sells whatever they can. The fund lost 44% of its money in August alone; $553 million on a single day.

The doom loop: losses → margin calls → forced selling into a falling market → prices pushed further against the remaining positions → more margin calls. By mid-September: ~$600 million of capital against $100+ billion of positions — an effective leverage near 167-to-1. The exact cascade our liquidation heatmap models, in government bonds, before crypto existed.

The toll: $4.6 billion lost in under four months. The Federal Reserve gathered 14 banks to inject $3.6 billion[13] — private money, but a rescue the Fed had to arrange because a fire-sale of the fund's positions threatened the whole financial system. (Source: Federal Reserve History; SEC-era estimates.)
Through our gauges (visible vs. invisible): the leverage ratio was knowable — 28:1[13] was in the fund's own reporting, the exact "real borrow vs. own money" number our board headlines. What was invisible: the assumption that markets stay orderly. Their models said a month like August 1998 was essentially impossible. The lesson our cushion teaches daily: the floor is real, the model of how far prices can move is a guess.
Exhibit 2 · May 2022 · Crypto — the death spiral
Terra / Luna — a "stable" coin that ran on belief

The story going in: UST, a "stablecoin" meant to always equal $1 — held there not by dollars in a bank, but by an algorithm trading against its sister coin LUNA. On top sat Anchor, a savings product paying ~20% a year, holding about 75% of all UST in existence. LUNA peaked at $119 in April 2022; the ecosystem was the third-largest in crypto.

The arithmetic that was visible the whole time: paying 20% on $14+ billion of deposits cost millions per day in subsidies — the project's own community voted to phase the rate down because everyone could see it wasn't sustainable. A 20% "safe" yield is a number that has to come from somewhere; here it came from newly printed value. That's checkable with grade-school math, before the crash, by anyone.

The trigger and the spiral: May 7, 2022 — large UST withdrawals nudged it below $1. Fear → withdrawals → the algorithm printed LUNA to absorb them → LUNA's price fell → more fear. In days, LUNA's supply exploded from about 1 billion coins toward 6 trillion and its price went from $80 to effectively zero. A bank run with no bank and no brakes — 24/7, no closing bell, no circuit breaker (the exact "every difference makes it worse" list from our field guide).

The toll: roughly $45–60 billion of Terra ecosystem value erased in days (~$40B LUNA + ~$18B UST at peak); over $400 billion knocked off the wider crypto market. South Korea alone estimated 28,000 of its citizens lost funds. Life savings went to zero on the belief a "stable" coin couldn't fall. Founder Do Kwon was later arrested and faces fraud charges (public record; he has pleaded not guilty).
Through our gauges (visible vs. invisible): visible — the unsustainable yield arithmetic, the concentration (75% of UST parked in one product chasing that yield = a crowd standing in one doorway), and once the run began, the on-chain outflows in real time. Invisible — the moment belief would break. Our needle would have graded "UST always equals $1" as what it was: an UNSUPPORTED CONCLUSION resting on the assumption that everyone keeps believing. No gauge can time a stampede; the honest read was that the doorway was narrow and the room was full.
Exhibit 3 · June 2022 · Crypto — the contagion
Three Arrows Capital — how one wreck sinks a fleet

The story going in: one of crypto's most respected hedge funds, managing about $10 billion at its peak, borrowing from nearly every big lender in the industry — often with little or no collateral, on reputation.

What happened: 3AC was heavily exposed to LUNA. When Terra died, the fund's losses ate its capital; within weeks it defaulted and went bankrupt. Because it had borrowed from everyone, its failure punched holes in its lenders — Voyager, Celsius, BlockFi, Genesis — and several of them fell too. That chain reached FTX by November.

The toll: a $10 billion fund to bankruptcy in weeks, then a cascade of lender bankruptcies freezing millions of ordinary customers' funds — people who never touched LUNA, never used leverage, and lost access anyway. (Public bankruptcy record.)
Through our gauges (visible vs. invisible): this is the exhibit about what no free gauge could see: 3AC's borrowing was private, off-chain, undisclosed — a leverage number that existed but was published nowhere. The lesson is the mirror image of the others: when the risk number is invisible, the honest reading is "unknown," not "fine." Our rule 4 ("we don't know yet" is a complete answer) is this exhibit's whole moral. Anyone who lent billions on reputation was treating an unknown as a fine.
Exhibit 4 · November 2022 · Crypto — the custody lie
FTX — the exchange that was betting the deposits

The story going in: the second-largest crypto exchange on Earth, fronted by celebrity endorsements, stadium naming rights, and a founder celebrated as the industry's responsible adult.

What happened: customer deposits — money people believed was sitting safe in their exchange accounts — had been funneled to the founder's trading firm, Alameda, and lost. When a leaked balance sheet showed Alameda propped up by FTX's own made-up token, a withdrawal run began. The exchange froze withdrawals and collapsed within days; the shortfall in customer money ran to roughly $8 billion. Founder Sam Bankman-Fried was convicted of fraud in 2023 and sentenced to 25 years (public court record).

The toll: millions of customers locked out of their funds for years of bankruptcy proceedings — including people who never traded at all, who just stored coins there.
Through our gauges (visible vs. invisible): the fraud itself was invisible — that's what fraud means. But notice which of our locked rules this exhibit wrote: an exchange balance is a promise; an on-chain wallet is a fact. Our whole tool exists on the fact side — reading a public chain nobody can privately misstate. And our identity rule ("never connects to your wallet, never touches money") is the structural opposite of the thing that failed here. FTX is why "not your keys, not your coins" stopped being a slogan and became evidence.
Exhibit 5 · July 2026 · Measured live by this project's own board — not borrowed from history
One wallet, 1,729 closed trades: a winning record that lost $5.86 million

What was measured (facts only, read from the public chain on July 11, 2026): a wallet surfaced in a random sample on our live board showed, across its recent trade window — 1,729 closed trades: 993 won, 736 lost — a 57% win rate. The same trades summed to a net result of −$5,858,448 after $8,521 in fees. Mostly traded: HYPE. Balance at reading: $60,455, no open bets.

Why it's in the museum: every other exhibit here needed a history book. This one came off our own instrument, from a random draw — the win-rate-versus-profit independence that our scorecard exists to teach, at a scale no textbook example matches. This trader won most of their trades. The majority of their trades were correct. And the sum of all of it was a nearly six-million-dollar loss, because the losing trades were, on average, far larger than the winning ones. Winning often and making money are different skills — here is the measurement.

The measured toll: −$5,858,448 net across the readable window, from a 57%-winning record. (Source: Hyperliquid public chain data, read live; reproducible by anyone with the wallet's address[26].)
The honest unknowns — named, not glossed (this is what non-biased means here): our reading sees a recent window of trades, not a lifetime — earlier results are invisible to us. We cannot see deposits or withdrawals, so we don't know what fraction of this trader's wealth the loss represents. We don't know who or what this wallet is: it could be one person's account, one leg of a larger strategy that profits elsewhere (a hedge we can't see), or an automated system where these losses are a known cost. The numbers are facts; every story about them is inference, and we assert none. What survives all the unknowns is the arithmetic itself: a majority-winning record and a multimillion-dollar loss coexisted in one wallet, which is impossible if win rate measured profitability — and that impossibility is the exhibit.
The pattern across every exhibit — one machine, four costumes:
1. A confident story ("geniuses can't lose" · "it's stable" · "they're too respected to check" · "he's the responsible one") — confidence sold in place of receipts, every time.
2. Borrowed size, visible (LTCM's 28:1) or hidden (3AC's private loans) — either way, no room to be wrong.
3. A crowd in one doorway — picture a packed theater with one exit. Nothing's wrong until someone smells smoke — then everyone needs the same door at the same second, and the door is the disaster. In markets the "door" is everyone holding the same trade (LTCM and its imitators), everyone chasing the same yield (75% of all UST packed into Anchor's 20%), or everyone trusting the same counterparty (half the industry lending to 3AC, millions of customers storing coins on FTX). The crowd itself is what makes the exit impossible.
4. A trigger from outside the model — every one of these players had a mental picture of "the worst that can realistically happen," and the thing that killed them wasn't in the picture. Russia refusing to pay its own debts (a default). A "stable" coin slipping off its dollar anchor (a depeg). A leaked spreadsheet. None of these were predictable from the price chart — which is exactly the point: the fatal blow usually comes from outside the data everyone is staring at. That's why "how much can I lose before I'm out?" (the cushion) protects you and "what do I predict happens?" doesn't.
5. Forced selling that feeds itself — the doom loop our liquidation gauges exist to make visible before it's personal.
And one more, quieter: in every single case, the arithmetic that mattered — the leverage ratio, the yield subsidy, the missing collateral — was either public and unread, or hidden and unasked-about. This museum exists so it gets read and asked about.

Where the live tool meets the living museum: the numbers on our board — real borrow vs. own money, room-to-fall, the safety floor, the scorecard — are exactly the numbers that told each of these stories in advance where they were public. The museum is what those numbers look like after the ending. And as of Exhibit 5, the two have met: the living museum now holds a piece of primary evidence our own instrument measured, found in a random draw — the flat wallets on the live board and the exhibits here are the same lesson at every size.
