Market positioning data

Eight big markets, read the same plain way: stocks, currencies, gold, rates, oil, gas, corn, and wheat. Tap the pull button, then open any market — each teaches how that market works, what its players are actually doing, how its common strategies use the data — and then shows you a live reading of who's positioned which way. It endorses nothing. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

One report powers every gauge on this page — and it's the safest data source we've ever used. Every Friday, the US government's futures regulator (the CFTC) publishes exactly who holds what in the futures markets — the Commitments of Traders report. It's government open data: free to everyone, no key, no terms to break. The honest limits, up front: it's weekly and lagged (Friday's report shows Tuesday's positions), it's a window into each market (futures traders, not everyone), and — same rule as our crypto gauges — positioning describes the crowd; it predicts nothing.

The one skill this whole page teaches: every market's data splits its players into two kinds, and the split is the reading.

Hedgers are real businesses using the market as insurance — a farmer locking in a wheat price, an airline locking in fuel, an exporter locking in a currency rate. They're not betting; they're offsetting a risk they already have.

Speculators are betting on the price itself. They provide the other side of the hedgers' insurance — and when they crowd hard onto one side, that's the same "packed trade" our crypto funding gauge (3a) and crowding gauge (3c) read.

This is the original version of the read we built for crypto — the COT report has been showing it since 1986, and it's where the "COT-style positioning" idea in our crypto gauge 3c came from in the first place.

Pull all four readings live. One tap fetches the latest weekly COT report for all eight markets straight from the CFTC's public API — no key, no sign-in, nothing between you and the regulator's own numbers. If any read fails, that gauge goes dark and says so; nothing is ever made up.

Source: publicreporting.cftc.gov (CFTC Socrata open-data API, Legacy futures-only report). Weekly; published Fridays with Tuesday's positions. Live government data, read straight from your phone — no key, no account.

Stocks — owning slices of businesses▸

What the market is: a share of stock is a small ownership slice of a real company. Its long-run value comes from the business's profits; its daily price comes from the crowd's mood about those profits.

The strategies people run here (described, not endorsed):

Indexing (own everything, sit still)
Buy the whole market cheaply and wait. How it uses data: almost none — that's the point. The scorekeepers (SPIVA) show ~92% of US stock funds failed to beat their benchmarks[5] over the 20 years through 2025. Its data need is one number: the fund's yearly cost.
Value investing (buy cheap slices)
Graham's game: find businesses priced below what they're worth. How it uses data: company report data — profits, debt, assets (free from SEC EDGAR). The read is business quality vs. price, not charts.
Trend / momentum (ride the crowd)
Buy what's rising, sell what's falling. How it uses data: price history and positioning — including the exact gauge below. Note the mirror: this strategy IS the herd, on purpose, with an exit plan. Most who try it have no exit plan.

The positioning read for stocks: the S&P 500 futures contract is where big money hedges and bets on the whole US stock market at once. When speculators there are heavily one-sided, the crowd is leaning.

Currencies (forex) — the price of money itself▸

What the market is: the biggest market on Earth — trading one country's money for another's. Prices move on interest rates, trade, and fear. Nobody "owns" a currency long-term the way you own a stock; every position is one money against another.

The strategies people run here (described, not endorsed):

Carry (earn the interest gap)
Hold the currency that pays higher interest, funded by one that pays less. How it uses data: central-bank interest rates (free from each central bank, and our FRED macro gauge reads the US side). The known failure mode: it earns small steadily, then loses big suddenly — "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller."
Trend following
Same as stocks — ride moves. Currencies trend when interest-rate gaps widen. How it uses data: price plus positioning like the gauge below.
Retail leveraged FX (the warning label)
Most small-account forex is done through CFDs — the same instrument as crypto perpetuals. The EU regulator's own data (ESMA, from our field guide): 74–89% of these accounts lose, and leverage is the named driver. Same machine, same result, different market.

The positioning read for currencies: euro futures show how speculators are leaning on the world's biggest currency pair (euro vs. dollar).

Commodities — real stuff: metal, fuel, food▸

What the market is: futures on physical things — gold, oil, wheat, copper. This is the market the COT report was literally invented for: farmers and factories hedging, speculators taking the other side.

The strategies people run here (described, not endorsed):

Hedging (the original purpose)
A wheat farmer sells futures at planting time to lock a price before harvest. Not a bet — insurance. How it uses data: the producer's own costs vs. today's lockable price.
Following the "smart money" (the folklore read)
A famous old read says: watch the hedgers, because they know their own business best. The honest status: studies disagree on whether this predicts anything — it's folklore with mixed evidence, and we say so rather than sell it. The data below shows you both sides' positions; the folklore is one interpretation, not a fact.
Gold as fear insurance
Gold pays nothing and builds nothing — its price is mostly a reading of fear and interest rates (when rates are high, holding a thing that pays nothing costs more). How it uses data: real interest rates (our FRED gauge, DFII10) plus positioning below.

The positioning read for commodities: gold futures — where fear, rates, and the crowd meet.

Bonds & interest rates — the market under every other market▸

What the market is: lending. A bond is a loan you can trade. Its price and its yield move opposite each other, and the interest rate on safe US government debt is the "price of waiting" that every other market is measured against — it's the Constraints force on our crypto dashboard, generalized.

The strategies people run here (described, not endorsed):

Laddering (the boring one)
Buy bonds maturing in steps (1yr, 2yr, 3yr…) and hold to maturity. How it uses data: just the posted yields. No prediction involved.
Curve reading (the famous signal)
Compare short-term vs long-term rates. When short rates rise above long rates (an "inverted curve"), recessions have often followed. The honest status: a real historical pattern with a real miss rate — a base-rate read, not a prophecy. Free from FRED/Treasury data.
Duration betting (the leveraged one)
Betting on the direction of rates using long bonds or futures. Rate moves hit long bonds hard in both directions — this is the bond market's version of leverage.

The positioning read for rates: 10-year Treasury note futures — where the biggest interest-rate bets in the world sit.

Fuel & food — the report's original home▸

These are the markets the COT report was invented for in the first place: farmers locking crop prices before harvest, refiners and utilities locking fuel costs — real businesses hedging real stuff, with speculators taking the other side. Everything you learned above applies unchanged; the crowd's lean here moves grocery bills and gas pumps, not just portfolios.

How to read the bars (one lesson, four markets): each bar shows the speculators' net lean — how far their "price up" bets outweigh their "price down" bets (or the reverse), as a share of all open positions. Center line = balanced. The hedgers sit roughly opposite by construction (someone has to take the other side).

What it does NOT mean: a crowded side is not a prediction. Crowds have stayed crowded for years and been right; crowds have flipped in a week. It's the same lesson as crypto funding: the reading tells you where the crowd is, so you're no longer the only one who can't see it. What it means next is exactly what nobody verified.

Who actually trades food and oil — and why you don't

These markets weren't built for investors at all. Futures exist so a farmer can lock in a wheat price before harvest and an airline can lock in fuel before winter — producers and users trading away risk, with speculators paid (in opportunity) to absorb it. That's the whole original machine, and it's why the COT report splits players into hedgers and speculators in the first place: the categories ARE the market's design.

Why ordinary people don't trade these directly — the sizes are industrial: one corn contract is 5,000 bushels (~130 tons); one oil contract is 1,000 barrels. Get the wrong side of a delivery month and you haven't lost an app balance — you've bought actual barrels. In April 2020 this stopped being a joke: with storage full, the expiring US oil future traded below zero — sellers paying buyers to take oil — and retail traders in brokered products discovered that "oil can't go below zero" was folklore. A price with warehouses attached obeys warehouse rules, not chart rules.

The onion that banned a market: in the 1950s two traders cornered the US onion futures market so completely — at one point holding most of the onions in Chicago — that prices collapsed to less than the cost of the bags they came in. Farmers were wrecked; Congress responded with the Onion Futures Act (1958), and onion futures are still illegal in the United States today. It's the cleanest historical proof that "the market" is a set of rules people wrote — and can rewrite when a market stops serving its purpose. (Historical record; the era's details are well documented.)

What changed since your grandparents' commodities: in the 2000s, index money arrived — pensions and funds buying baskets of commodity futures as an "asset class," rolling contracts forever without ever wanting a single barrel. This financialization is why the speculator share in the gauges above is structurally bigger than in old textbooks, and it's an honest open question in the research: how much does investment money move food and fuel prices? Both "a lot" and "less than headlines claim" have serious defenders — we show the positioning and leave the verdict unclaimed.

The plain takeaway: for a household, exposure to commodities happens at the pump and the grocery store already. The gauges above aren't an invitation to trade corn — they're a window into a professional risk-transfer machine, shown so the machine can't be mythologized to you.
