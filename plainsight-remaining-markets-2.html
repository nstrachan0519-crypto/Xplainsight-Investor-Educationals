The remaining markets — every one, in plain words

Seven markets to complete the map: the betting contracts, the buildings, the boring cash, the physical stuff, the money-vs-money game, the rest of the world, and the invisible force under all of them. Each one: what it is, how it really works, and the accepted strategies argued for AND against with the best published evidence we could find — both sides at full strength. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

Options & derivatives — contracts about prices

What an option is: a contract, not a thing. A call is the right (not the duty) to BUY something at a set price before a deadline; a put is the right to SELL at a set price. You pay a fee (the premium) for that right. Think of a call as a refundable deposit on a house: pay $1,000 for the right to buy at $300k within 90 days — if the house jumps to $350k you profit hugely off $1,000; if it doesn't, you lose exactly the $1,000. That built-in leverage — big exposure from small money — is the whole attraction and the whole danger.

The three honest facts under every option: (1) every contract has a deadline, and time itself eats the buyer's value every single day (theta) — an option is a melting ice cube; (2) it's a zero-sum contract — the buyer's win is exactly the seller's loss, minus fees for both, so unlike owning stocks there is no rising tide that lifts everyone; (3) options were invented as insurance (a farmer locking a price, an investor protecting a portfolio), and insurance has a known economics: on average, the BUYER of insurance pays more than it's worth, and the SELLER collects that overpayment — which explains almost everything in the research below.

The case FOR (as its advocates and the data state it): defined-risk protection is real — buying a put on your portfolio is genuine insurance with a known max cost. Selling options harvests a documented premium: in the retail data, option sales averaged about +20% per trade while purchases lost, and covered-call income strategies have decades of institutional use. And the newest data cuts against the "reckless gambler" stereotype: Cboe's own analysis found over 95% of retail zero-day trades used limited-risk[4] structures where max loss is known at entry — much retail flow is more disciplined than the headlines say.
The case AGAINST (the published record, and it's heavy): India's market regulator ran the largest natural experiment ever — 93% of retail options-and-futures traders lost money over three years (FY22–FY24)[1], with roughly 1% earning meaningful profit, and transaction costs alone consuming fortunes. Europe's regulator reads the same from its own books: ESMA's standardized broker disclosures found 74–89% of retail CFD accounts lose[2] money — different continent, different instrument, same machine. US academic studies agree from every angle: the average retail option trade loses ~1% at the moment it's placed; option purchases average −4%[3]; same-day-expiry (0DTE) trades — now over 75% of retail index-option volume — do worst of all, bleeding an estimated $350,000 per retail day[3] in aggregate; complex multi-leg trades lost ~16% over three days; and trading around earnings, the most popular moment, multiplies the losses 3×. The mechanism isn't mystery — it's the melting ice cube plus wide spreads plus insurance economics: most retail flow BUYS the insurance that reliably overprices.

The honest synthesis: the buy side and sell side of options are different businesses with opposite base rates — buyers pay the insurance markup for lottery-shaped outcomes; sellers collect it for steady income punctuated by rare disasters (the +20% average includes sellers who eventually meet the one move that erases years — "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller" is the sell-side's own proverb). The research dispute is real too: Cboe-sponsored work argues the loss studies mis-identify who counts as "retail." But even the friendliest reading leaves the SEBI result standing: when a regulator counted every actual account, 93 in 100 lost. Options are professional insurance machinery, learnable and legitimate — and the published record says the typical newcomer is the customer, not the insurer.

Real estate & REITs — the market people live inside

Two very different things share this name. Direct property: you buy a building — the only investment most people ever leverage 5-to-1 (a 20% down payment) with society's blessing, and the only one you can live in. REITs (real estate investment trusts): companies that own hundreds of buildings and are legally required to pay out at least 90% of their taxable income to shareholders — buildings turned into something you can buy like a stock, in any amount, and sell in seconds.

The weighing machine verdict: real estate HAS a scale under it — rent. That makes it a fundamentals asset like stocks (income, costs, a 10-Q if it's a REIT), not a belief asset like gold. Its special traits: rents tend to ride inflation upward (a real hedge), but the asset runs on borrowed money at every level, which makes the whole market a creature of interest rates — our Constraints force again, wearing a roof.

FOR direct ownership: the leverage is uniquely cheap and non-callable — no one force-sells your house because its price dipped, the exact mercy no exchange ever grants; you can improve the asset with your own hands; rents rise with inflation; and the forced-savings effect of a mortgage is, in the behavioral data, how a huge share of ordinary families actually built their wealth. FOR REITs: the rent-collecting business without tenants, toilets, or a mortgage; instant diversification across hundreds of buildings; decades of returns competitive with stocks; and honest daily pricing.
AGAINST direct ownership: it's the ultimate concentration — one asset, one street, one roof, often several times your net worth; costs are relentless (taxes, insurance, repairs eat rent); selling takes months and ~6% in fees; and 2008 stands as the museum-grade proof that "housing never falls nationally" was a forever-claim like any other. AGAINST REITs: they trade like stocks and CRASH like stocks (2008: many fell more than the market), so the diversification is weaker exactly when wanted; they're rate-sensitive both ways; and the 90%-payout rule means they grow by issuing shares — check the dilution lesson from the company-reading page.

Cash instruments — the forgotten asset class

The unglamorous truth: cash-like holdings — high-yield savings, CDs, money market funds, Treasury bills — are a real asset class, the only one whose price never falls, and the one everyone skips past. Their job is never growth. Their jobs: the emergency fund (Ramsey's step one, and the single most agreed-upon idea in all of personal finance — cash between you and disaster means never selling investments at the bottom to fix a transmission); known near-term spending; and dry powder that lets every other plan survive.

FOR: government insurance up to legal limits makes bank cash the one holding with a true floor; T-bills are the actual "risk-free rate" every market is measured against; and behaviorally, a cash buffer is what stops the panic-sell — the cheapest psychology upgrade money can buy.
AGAINST (holding too much): inflation is a guaranteed slow leak — cash never loses dollars and almost always loses buying power; over decades it's the worst-performing major asset in history by a landslide; and "waiting in cash for the crash" is a documented losing strategy on average — the market-timing literature keeps finding that missed good days cost more than dodged bad ones. Cash is a shield, priced like a shield: it wins nothing.

Physical commodities — oil, gas, grain, copper

What it is: markets in actual stuff, run through futures (our markets pack already reads the crowd here via the COT gauges). The plain-words physics: nobody stores oil in their garage, so almost all investment access is through rolling futures contracts — and that roll has a hidden cost or gain (contango and its opposite) that can make "oil went up but my oil fund didn't" a genuine and common experience. The commodity's price and a commodity FUND's return are cousins, not twins.

FOR: commodities historically spike during exactly the inflation shocks that hurt stocks and bonds together (1970s, 2022) — one of the few true diversifiers for that specific storm; and the markets exist for real hedgers, so the machinery is old, deep, and honest about its purpose.
AGAINST: no weighing machine — a barrel earns nothing and builds nothing, so long-run returns after roll costs have been poor for passive holders (the famous studies favoring commodity allocations used past decades whose conditions may not repeat — a survivorship-of-eras problem); the roll cost is a quiet permanent leak in many funds; and the segment is a professional hedgers-vs-speculators arena where the retail buyer is usually the third party paying both.

Currency risk — the game every saver is already in

The part nobody tells savers: you don't opt into the currency market — holding any currency IS a position. Every dollar, euro, or peso you own is a bet on that money keeping its buying power against all the others. The markets pack covers trading currencies; this section covers the saver's honest questions: what happens to my money if my home currency weakens (imported prices rise — you feel it as inflation), and does owning foreign assets protect me (partly — foreign stocks carry foreign-currency exposure inside them, a hidden diversifier or a hidden risk depending on the decade).

FOR paying attention (not trading): unhedged international holdings have historically cushioned home-currency weakness; people in countries with fragile currencies have preserved savings through hard assets and foreign exposure — the one context where even the sober literature respects currency diversification as self-defense.
AGAINST doing much about it: for savers in major reserve currencies, decades of data show currency swings mostly wash out over long horizons; hedging costs real money forever to remove a risk that historically netted near zero; and short-term currency prediction has one of the worst professional track records of any forecasting field — if the experts' base rate is a coin flip, the saver's edge is not better.

International & emerging stocks — the rest of the scale

The plain fact: the US is roughly 60% of world stock value — meaning a US-only investor ignores about 40% of all the businesses on Earth. Home bias — everyone overweighting their own country — is one of the best-documented behaviors in finance, and every country's investors do it. Emerging markets add the faster-growing, rockier economies, with a famous catch: decades of data show a country's GDP growth and its stock returns are only loosely connected — the growth story is real, and it has repeatedly failed to reach shareholders (dilution, governance, and prices that already believed the story).

FOR going global: the whole logic of indexing extended honestly — own ALL the businesses, not just your country's; the record's Japan card is the case study of what a one-country bet can do to a lifetime (a Japanese home-bias investor in 1989 waited 35 years to break even); and leadership rotates — the US and international have traded decade-long winning streaks all century, and nobody has reliably called the handoffs.
AGAINST (as its serious critics argue): US multinationals already earn ~40% of revenue abroad — you may own the world through them; international adds currency swings, higher costs, and governance risk; the US structural argument (deepest markets, strongest shareholder law) is real; and Bogle himself — patron saint of owning everything — famously held that US-only was enough. When the index camp's own founder is the dissent, the debate is honest on both sides.

Inflation — the force under every market

What it actually is: not "prices going up" — money's buying power going down. The distinction matters: your account balance never shows the leak, which is why inflation is the stealthiest force in finance. At 3% a year, money halves its buying power in ~24 years; at 7%, in ~10 (rule of 72). Every "return" you ever see is nominal until you subtract it — the only return you can eat is the real one.

How it touches every section above: it's why cash leaks, why bonds' fixed coupons are its favorite prey (and why TIPS exist), why gold-believers believe, why rents and commodity spikes hedge it, why the Fed's fight against it (rates) is the ruler that re-prices everything — and why the single most important habit this whole compendium can teach is asking of every number: "is that before or after inflation?" Most of the impressive numbers in financial marketing are before. Most of real life is after.
