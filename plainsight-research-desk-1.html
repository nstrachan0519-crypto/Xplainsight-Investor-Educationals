The research desk

Real investors don't guess at squiggles — they observe, request evidence, form a thesis, state their confidence, then commit. This desk makes you practice exactly that, on real dated market history with real prices on the axes. You are graded on two things only: calibration (does your sureness match your hit rate) and process (did you look at the evidence before deciding). Being right is not one of the grades — a good decision can lose and a bad one can win, and this desk never confuses the two.

An honest limit, up front: this is a close-price line chart, not a full trading terminal. No candlesticks here on purpose — that specific claim gets its own dedicated, harder test on the Candle Test page. Volume is real and included for crypto & metals (same data our funding gauges use) — but it doesn't meaningfully exist for currencies, stocks, or rates the way it does for a single exchange, so we say so rather than fake it. Real professional readers do use more inputs than this desk shows; what this desk trains is the habit — look before you decide — which transfers to any chart, however much data it has.
The resolution rule, written before you play (so it can't bend afterward): over the hidden 30 days, comparing the last shown price to the final price — UP / DOWN means it moved past that market's pre-set cutoff · SIDEWAYS means it stayed inside. Cutoffs are fixed per market in advance, because a "big move" differs by market: crypto ±10% (30 days) · metals ±3% (30 days) · currencies ±1.5% (30 days) · stocks ±10% (12 months) · rates ±10% (12 months). Same rule for every round, forever.

Pick your market — crypto, metals, and currencies play 30-day windows from live data; stocks and rates play 12-month windows drawn from up to 150 YEARS of public-domain history (your window might be 1929, 1966, or last year — that's the point):

34% = barely better than the three-way coin flip · 99% = certain. Be honest — the grade is about whether your sureness matches your hit rate.

What this teaches (the honest version): across many rounds, most people's direction-reads land near the random baseline (~33%) — that's not an insult, it's the finding from every scorekeeper study in our field guide: short-run direction is close to a coin flip, even for professionals. The skill this simulator actually trains is the rarer one: knowing how sure to be. If you say 80% and hit 40%, the market didn't beat you — your confidence did. And remember what the simulator leaves out: in real trading, every round also subtracts fees, and leverage turns wrong-but-recoverable into wiped-out.

What "the mirror" means (it ends every room): after testing everyone else's claims, we turn the same test on ourselves — in two words: self-check. Three questions, every time: what did this page actually prove, what can it NOT know, and did it ever slip from showing you what IS into telling you what to DO? A page anywhere else that never holds a mirror to itself is telling you something too.
The mirror, as always: every window here is finished history — nothing on this page is a prediction, a signal, or advice about any market now. Your rounds live only on this page and vanish when you close it: no account, no storage, nothing sent anywhere. It's a gym, not a broker.
