The signal test

The famous chart signals people cite, sell, and swear by — put to the test against up to 150 years of real market history, right now, on your phone. No cherry-picked decade, no survivor examples: every time each signal ever fired, and what the next 12 months actually did, next to the base rate it has to beat. The scoring rule is printed before any test runs, and it's the same for every signal, forever. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

Markets owe no one patterns. Every famous signal earns its reputation here by surviving the same public, pre-registered test — or it doesn't. And one boundary holds throughout: this engine measures whether a pattern historically repeated; it cannot determine why. History shows what happened — never the reason.

Why only 11 signals, when thousands exist? Because we don't try to cover them all — we couldn't, and a list that long would just be a new kind of noise. We test the most famous, most-cited ones properly, and we built the machine so YOU can test any other: the bring-your-own-CSV loader runs a claim you define through the same seven checks. The lesson was never "here are the signals that work." It's "here is how to check any signal, forever." That scales to thousands even though our list doesn't.
Where do these signals come from? Every signal on this page is a calculation made from past prices only — no earnings, no news, no company information, nothing about the future. Indicators don't "see" anything; they rearrange what already happened into a pattern, and traders then believe the pattern means something. This page asks the only question that matters about that belief: when the pattern appeared in history, did the market actually do better afterward?

The pre-registered test (fixed before the data loads, so it can't bend): no signal gets a lone pass/fail. Each faces seven independent checks — beating our deliberately-nonsense signals, the unconditional baseline, and a random-entry dart; reaching significance by a permutation test (which makes no bell-curve assumption); surviving multiple-comparison correction; keeping the same edge across three historical eras (sign stability); and holding on independent non-overlapping windows. Passes 6–7 = strong historical evidence; 4–5 = moderate; 2–3 = mixed; 0–1 = little — always "historical evidence," never "true": history can show what repeated, not what will. Signals too rare for statistics get their full record shown instead of a fake verdict. Tap any check name for a plain-English meaning.

🧪 The tests we run on our own tests (overlap, honest limits) ▾ tap to open
Rule 0, applied to our own method (the tests we run on the tests): the biggest weakness in this kind of study is that overlapping 12-month windows repeat each other — measure Jan→Jan and Feb→Feb and you've counted almost the same year twice, which makes naive confidence numbers too rosy. We handle that two independent ways and show both: Angle 11 re-runs each signal on only non-overlapping yearly windows (fewer fires, but each a genuinely separate story), and Angle 12 is a planned second angle — a standard overlap correction called Newey-West, which keeps all the data but honestly widens the uncertainty math to account for the repeats. It is not implemented in this version; naming it before it's real would be exactly the kind of overclaim this project exists to catch in others. Until it ships, non-overlapping windows (Angle 11) is the correction actually running — and a signal that survives all seven checks on that alone is real evidence on its own. Remaining honest limits: 150 years is really only ~150 truly-independent years; "appears true" means "survived every stress test we could build," never "proven" or "will work for you"; and this is monthly data — the fast signals day-traders use live below it (see the refusal list above and the crypto candle test for where daily data lets us look closer).
📐 Full method & every parameter (so anyone can reproduce or refute this)

Data: built-in run uses the public-domain Shiller monthly S&P series (1871→), price-only, no dividends. Your own CSV runs the identical engine at its own resolution.

Signal windows (fixed in advance): moving averages 10-month & 2-month; momentum 12-month; RSI-14; MACD 12/26/9; buy-dip at −10% and −20% from running peak; CAPE from Shiller's own column, threshold 30. A signal "fires" only on the bar it first becomes true, using only closed data up to that bar — audited line-by-line for look-ahead (decision inputs never touch the outcome window).

Outcome: the next 12 months' price return (monthly) or ~3 months (daily uploads). Controls: (1) unconditional — all windows; (2) random-entry dart — same number of entries chosen at random, 3,000 repeats, 95% band; (3) nonsense group — four arbitrary rules that must score at the base rate. The old "same-state" control was removed as biased (it compared a buy-signal's months against mostly-down months — an unfair tailwind).

Significance: a permutation test (4,000 reshuffles) — no normal-curve assumption, because market returns are fat-tailed and skewed and a plain z-test would be technically wrong here. Multiple-comparison correction: Benjamini-Hochberg across all tested signals. Overlap: handled by also testing non-overlapping windows. Eras: history split in thirds; a real edge keeps its sign in all three.

External review, credited honestly: this engine's v4 upgrades — the evidence-language verdicts, effect sizes, bootstrap intervals, economic-significance line, regime breakdown, parameter robustness, and the cross-market path — came from an external AI peer review of our method. Per our own rules: praise is not validation and earned nothing; the suggestions were adopted because each makes the test harder to fool, and each was verified on known answers before shipping. Still queued from that review: Bayes factors and parameter-stability heatmaps.

What we cannot fix, stated plainly (Rule 0 on ourselves):

• Menu survivorship: we test famous signals — famous partly because they once seemed to work. The forgotten failures aren't here. The nonsense group is our partial defense; it shows the noise floor honestly.
• The market itself is a survivor: the US market is the one that didn't get closed, hyperinflated, or nationalized. Signals from the winning market can't tell you they'd have worked on the ones that died.
• "150 years" is really 4–5 different markets: pre-Fed gold standard, interwar, Bretton Woods, post-1971 fiat, modern electronic — different money, different index composition (the S&P wasn't 500 stocks until 1957), different participants and laws. A signal that only worked in one era is likely an artifact of that era. That's why the three-era sign check matters.
• Reflexivity: a signal that worked before it was famous can stop working because it became famous and the crowd traded it away.
• No costs, taxes, or slippage modeled — real trading only lowers every result. "Beats the dart" is not "tradeable."
• Independent years are few: 150 years of overlapping windows is closer to ~150 truly independent observations — humbling, and the reason nothing here is ever "proven."

🚫 What this page refuses to grade, and why ▾ tap to open
What this page refuses to grade, and why (honesty about our own limits): candlestick patterns, intraday support/resistance, scalping signals — they live below monthly resolution, and a test that can't see a claim can't judge it (the account-level results of the people who trade them are on our options page). Volume signals — this dataset has no volume column, and we don't fake data. Fibonacci retracements and Elliott waves — as commonly used, they're flexible enough to fit any outcome after the fact, which makes them unfalsifiable — and a claim that can't be wrong can't be right either. That's not a small result; it's the most useful one on the page.
⚠️ The trap this page exists to expose (read before believing ANY indicator) ▾ tap to open
The trap this page is built to expose (read before believing ANY indicator, including ours): test twenty rules on one history and one will shine by pure luck — that's the multiple-comparisons problem, and it's how most sold indicators are born. Our defenses: the verdict rule was fixed in advance, it's identical for every signal, we publish the losers next to the winners, and the whole test re-runs live on public data anyone can download. Also stated plainly: no trading costs are modeled (costs only make every signal worse), returns are price-only on both sides of every comparison (fair, favors no one), and "held up over 150 years of history" is evidence about the past — the crowd can trade away tomorrow what worked yesterday, and does.
🪞 The mirror — our self-check ▾ tap to open

What "the mirror" means (it ends every room): after testing everyone else's claims, we turn the same test on ourselves. In two words: self-check. Three questions, every time — what did this page actually prove, what can it NOT know, and did it ever slip from showing you what IS into telling you what to DO? If a page you read anywhere else never holds a mirror to itself, that silence is information too.

This page's mirror: it grades famous claims against public history with one fixed rule — it does not tell you to trade anything, and a signal that held up here is not a promise, an edge for you personally, or a recommendation. What it proves is narrower and more valuable: the checking was always possible, free, on a phone. Everyone who sold you an indicator could have shown you this table. Ask why they didn't.
