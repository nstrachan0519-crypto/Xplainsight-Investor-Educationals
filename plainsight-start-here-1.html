🌱 Start here — before any market

The best returns most people will ever get aren't in markets — and almost nobody selling market content says so, because there's nothing to sell you. This room is the hallway before every classroom in this app: the moves that beat stocks, crypto, and gold combined, in order. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

The order of operations — free money first

Step 1 — Kill expensive debt. A credit card charging 24% interest is a machine running backwards: paying it off is a guaranteed 24% return. No stock, coin, or fund on this entire site offers a guaranteed 24%. Nothing here beats this step; do the math on your own cards first.
Step 2 — Take the match. Many jobs offer a retirement match: put money in, and your employer adds more — often 50 cents or a full dollar per dollar you save, up to a limit. That's an instant 50–100% return, before the money even touches a market. Leaving it unclaimed is turning down part of your pay.
Step 3 — Build the cushion. An emergency fund (a few months of bills, in boring cash) isn't an investment — it's what makes every other investment survivable. Without it, the first surprise bill forces you to sell at the worst moment — which is exactly the wreck our bonds page calls sequence risk, in miniature.
Step 4 — Only now, markets. Money that clears steps 1–3 — money you won't need for years — is the only money that belongs in anything else this app teaches.

The engine: compounding (and its evil twin)

Compounding is earning returns on your returns — a snowball rolling downhill, growing by what it already gathered. The pocket trick: the rule of 72 — divide 72 by a yearly return to get roughly how many years your money takes to double. At 8%, about 9 years. At 2%, about 36.

The evil twin: debt compounds the same way, against you. That 24% credit card doubles what you owe roughly every 3 years (72 ÷ 24). The same snowball, rolling onto you. This one sentence is why Step 1 outranks every market.

The quiet corollary nobody sells: early on, how much you save matters far more than what it earns. Saving $200/month at a mediocre return beats saving $50/month at a brilliant one for years and years — pure arithmetic. Beginners agonize over picking winners when the pick barely matters yet; the deposit does.

Four words people mix up (most losses live in the mix-up)

Saving — money kept safe for known, nearish needs. Goal: don't lose it. Tool: bank accounts, cash-like things.
Investing — buying pieces of productive things (businesses, loans) and holding for years, expecting growth because the things produce. Goal: long-term growth. Risk: real, but paired with time.
Speculating — betting on price moves themselves, often shorter-term, where your gain is mostly someone else's loss. Not evil — but it's a competition, and our whole app measures how that competition usually ends for newcomers.
Gambling — betting where the odds are known to be against you, for entertainment. Honest gambling knows it's gambling. The expensive version is gambling that believes it's investing.

The lesson: each game has different rules, different time horizons, and different odds. The most expensive mistake in markets isn't picking wrong — it's playing one game while believing you're playing another. Every room in this app quietly asks: which of the four is this, really?

The unfair math of losing

Losses are heavier than gains — by arithmetic, not psychology. Lose 50% and you need +100% just to get back to even. Lose 90% and you need +900%. This is why every verified winner on our bookshelf obsesses over not losing big before anything else, and why the museum's wrecks never came back. Try it on the flight simulator: one deep hole eats many good reads.

Your one real edge

Time is the small investor's only genuine advantage — and it's real. A professional fund manager gets fired for one bad year, so they can't truly wait. You can. Decades of patience is the one strategy the professionals are structurally banned from using and you aren't. Notice it's also the cheapest one.

Boring, powerful, US-specific (with the honest caveat)

Tax-protected accounts — in the US: 401(k), IRA, HSA — are wrappers that shield growth from taxes. The same investment inside vs. outside the wrapper can differ by a huge amount over decades, for zero extra risk. Caveat, said plainly: these are US rules, they change, and the limits move yearly — verify current numbers at irs.gov before acting. Other countries have their own versions.

What's actually protected — three words people dangerously confuse: FDIC protects bank deposits if the bank fails. SIPC protects brokerage accounts if the broker fails — it never, ever protects against your investments losing value. Crypto held on an exchange: neither — no insurance, as the FTX case in our living museum shows in the hardest way.

Two honest mechanics before you go

Automatic investing (dollar-cost averaging): putting in the same amount every payday is behaviorally brilliant — it removes the decision, which removes the panic and the FOMO. The honest math note: when people have a lump sum, investing it all at once has historically beaten drip-feeding it in most of the time[29], because markets rise more often than they fall. So: automate your paychecks (behavior win), and know the "always DCA a windfall" folklore is a comfort choice, not a math one — both are legitimate, for different reasons.

Rebalancing: once in a while, nudging your mix back to plan (selling a bit of what grew, adding to what shrank). It's maintenance, not strategy — like rotating tires. Mentioned once here so the word never scares you again.

Two doors we point at but don't teach: insurance (protecting your income and family from disasters) and estate basics (what happens to your stuff). Both matter more than most market decisions and both are outside this school — this app teaches markets, and pretending otherwise would break its own rules. Real financial-planning resources or a verified fee-only professional (see our Getting Help room) cover them.
