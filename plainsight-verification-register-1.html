Verification register

Every tool we used, whether that tool has itself been checked, every test we ran, and every claim we made — including the ones we got wrong and withdrew. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

Why this page exists. A finding is only as good as the instrument that produced it. We spent a long time reaching conclusions using tools we had never checked. When we finally checked them, we found the checking tools had bugs of their own — so some of our conclusions had to be withdrawn.

This page is the receipt for all of it. Every claim carries the number that produced it. A claim with no number is an opinion, and we treat that as a bug.

2 · The test methods

Nine methods. Each answers a different question, and several exist only because an earlier method missed something.

The nine methods ▾ tap to open

1 · Known-answer markets. Build fake data where we already know the truth — some with no pattern, some with a pattern we planted — and check the engine agrees.

2 · Many market types, never one. Eight kinds of fake market. Our first version used one, and a reviewer pointed out that a result from one simulator describes the simulator, not the engine.

3 · Mutation testing. Break the code on purpose and confirm the tests scream. A test suite that passes a knowingly broken engine is decoration.

4 · Comparison against software we did not write. Run the same numbers through SciPy — built and checked by thousands of people worldwide. This is your cheapest check on us, because it does not require trusting us.

5 · Rules that must always hold. Checked against many random datasets, not a few hand-picked ones: a probability must stay between 0 and 1; identical input must give identical output; an impossibly good result must give a tiny p-value; an impossibly bad one a large one.

6 · Random placement in real history. Scatter fake signals at random points in the real S&P record. No real edge can exist, but every real feature — crashes, calm spells, trends — is kept. This caught things the fake markets missed.

7 · Splitting causes apart. Before accepting any explanation, vary the suspected causes separately and together. Our first, tidier explanation turned out to be half right.

8 · Threshold sweeps. Change one cut-off, hold the rest, count how many verdicts flip. Separates a number that matters from one that does nothing.

9 · Working out the luck rate exactly. Compute how often a check passes by chance alone. Arithmetic did what no amount of judgement had: it proved one of our shipped checks was a coin flip.

Claims we withdrew

Listed because a record showing only what survived is an advertisement.

What we saidWhat checking showed
"False alarms fell from 46.7% to 3.5%"Measured on one fake market. Across eight, the same engine ranged 1.7%–8.3%
"Block methods are miscalibrated at 0.29%"True only under one harsh setup. Normally 0–2.7% — cautious, not broken
"Lower false-alarm rate means missing real things"Not a law. One method had a lower rate and equal detection (2.9% vs 17.9%, both 100%)
"Detected 5 of 5 planted edges, so the scoring works"The planted effect was enormous. The test could only have seen an elephant
"The uncorrected method over-claims"It under-claimed. Written before running it; the data said the opposite
"Months mostly behave independently"On real prices they agree 65% of the time, not the 50% predicted
"A short outcome window would expose the timing bug"Measured 0 difference at 1, 2 and 6 bars — the opposite of the prediction
"Maximum entropy is a valid conservative test"It is inapplicable. Its p-values sit at 0.41–0.55 always; it can never reject anything
The pattern behind every withdrawal. Not one was a made-up fact. Every single one was a true thing carrying more weight than it had earned — a real result quoted beyond its range, a real method judged from one setup, a conclusion written before the number arrived. That is the hardest failure to see from the inside, because nothing about it looks wrong. It is why Rule 0B exists.

4 · Not yet checked

Named here rather than left for you to discover:

· One deliberate bug still escapes our tests. An off-by-one in signal timing. Two explanations were tested and both were wrong, so the cause is unknown. We do not claim our tests detect mis-timing.

· The daily engine has never been mutation-tested and its cut-off numbers have never been audited for where they came from.

· The 150-year price series has never been tested — only 13 years of one market. So the daily engine's era-stability check is unverified.

· Four app pages have never been swept at all: the candle test, the markets pack, an older board version, and part of the research desk.

· No out-of-sample holdout exists anywhere. Every test uses history we have already seen. By our own rules that caps us at "measured, not predictive."

· Four of eight luck-test families are conservative in ways we have not fully explained, and one is inapplicable.

5 · How to catch us

Don't trust us completely. We are transparent for exactly this reason.

A person will miss 100% of the problems they never thought to check for.

That applies to us as much as anyone. Every fault on this page was found by attacking our own work, or by an outside reviewer, or by being forced to run a test instead of reasoning about it. None was found by anything feeling uncertain. Confidence does not detect bugs — it ends the search.
