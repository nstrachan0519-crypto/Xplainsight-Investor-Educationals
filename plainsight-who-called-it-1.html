Who called it

Famous market predictions, each frozen at the moment it was made — then scored by the only judge that counts: what actually happened. Nobody keeps a record like this, because nobody profits from it and everyone powerful is embarrassed by it. That's exactly why it's ours to keep. The rule of the ground: it holds confident wrongness up to the light from both directions — bulls and bears sit here side by side, because the lesson is that confidence doesn't correlate with accuracy, not that either side is wrong. Tap any underlined word for a plain meaning.

How each record is written: the call as documented (who, when, where) · what reality did · how long resolution took · and how our evidence needle would have graded the claim on the day it was made — because a prediction's honesty can be judged before its outcome. Several of these people were brilliant, and the record says so; being smart and being calibrated are different things, which is the whole cemetery in one sentence.

Bullish call · missed
The permanently high plateau[15]
"Stock prices have reached what looks like a permanently high plateau."
Irving Fisher, Yale economist — then America's most famous — in a New York speech, printed in the New York Times, October 16, 1929.

Fisher wasn't a fraud — he was one of the great economists of his century, using data and a mathematical method he'd spent decades building. He also practiced what he preached: heavily in stocks, heavily on margin.

Reality: the crash began about two weeks later. By mid-November the market had lost nearly half its value; by the 1932 bottom, 89%. The Dow did not see its 1929 level again until November 1954 — twenty-five years. Fisher himself was ruined, doubling down on the way down, borrowing from family to meet margin calls. (Sources: Federal Reserve History; contemporaneous NYT record.)

The needle, on the day: "permanently" is a claim about all future time made from eight years of rising prices — UNSUPPORTED CONCLUSION, gradeable as such before the crash, by anyone, from the word alone. The word "looks like" was the honest part; "permanently" was the confidence talking.

Bullish call · missed
Nikkei 80,000 by 1995
In 1989, with Japan's Nikkei index near 39,000 and priced around 70 years' worth of earnings, the country's largest brokerage published a report projecting the index would reach 80,000 by 1995[16].
Nomura research report, 1989 — the peak of the Japanese bubble. (Contemporaneous brokerage record, cited in later reviews.)
Reality: the Nikkei peaked weeks later near 39,000 and then fell for over a decade, losing most of its value. It did not merely miss 80,000 by 1995 — it took until 2024, thirty-five years later, just to climb back to its own 1989 peak. This is our buy-and-hold deep dive's Japan chapter, seen from the prediction side.

The needle, on the day: a doubling target justified by the same reasoning that had already driven prices to historic extremes — the evidence for the claim WAS the price itself, a circle. STRETCHED at best. Note who published it: the institution earning fees on the buying. The needle grades claims, not motives — but the field guide says always ask who's paid when the claim comes true.

Companion card — why Japan's market did that (the 5-minute version):

1. Cheap money got turned on. In 1985 the big countries made a deal that pushed Japan's currency way up, which hurt its exporters. To soften that, Japan's central bank made borrowing very cheap — and all that cheap borrowed money poured into stocks and land.

2. A loop that fed itself. Banks lent money using land as the promise ("if you don't pay, we take the land"). The loans pushed land prices up. Higher land prices meant the promises looked bigger — so banks lent even more. Round and round. People joked that the Emperor's garden in Tokyo was "worth more than all of California."

3. Nobody was selling. Big companies owned each other's shares as a loyalty custom, so most shares never came up for sale — buying pushed prices up fast because so little was available.

4. The story. "Japan's economy is a new kind — the old price rules don't apply here." At the top, stocks cost about 70 years' worth of the profits behind them. Normal is more like 15–20 years' worth.

5. The turn. When Japan's central bank finally raised borrowing costs in 1989–90, the loop ran backwards: land fell → the banks' promises were suddenly worth less → banks were stuck with dying loans for a decade → prices kept sinking → and Japan's aging, shrinking population meant fewer new buyers behind them, year after year. That's why recovery took 35 years instead of 3: the bubble wasn't just in prices — it was wired into the banks and the population itself.

Could America be next? The honest both-sides: today the US market's price-vs-ten-years-of-profits gauge (the CAPE) reads around 40–42 (July 2026) — near its all-time record of 44.2, which was set in December 1999[14], right before the dot-com crash, against a long-run middle value near 16. And there's a confident "new era" story attached to a handful of giant companies. Those are real bubble ingredients, visibly present. But most of Japan's deadliest plumbing is absent here: US banks don't run on a land-loan loop and are required to hold more cushion since 2008; there's no everyone-owns-everyone share web; the US population still grows; big US companies earn worldwide; and America's central bank has shown it intervenes fast rather than letting prices sink for a decade — which is why the 2000 and 2008 crashes recovered in years, not decades. What history fairly supports: expensive markets have usually paid poorly over the following ten years, and sometimes crashed — but "expensive" has never told anyone when, and Japan's 35-year freezer needed ingredients America mostly lacks. "It's 1989 Japan" and "it can't happen here" are both forever-claims the needle grades hardest. The question that actually protects you is the museum's: not "what do I predict?" but "how much can I lose before I'm out — and does my plan survive being wrong for years?"

Bull · "right" 22 years late — which is its own lesson
Dow 36,000, "over the next few years"
A 1999 best-selling book argued the Dow — then around 10,000 — was wildly undervalued and would rise to 36,000 within a few years, because stocks would soon be seen as no riskier than bonds.
"Dow 36,000," James Glassman[17] & Kevin Hassett, 1999 — serious, credentialed authors; a contemporaneous Harvard review called the argument "not as laughable as it sounds" while rejecting the confidence.
Reality: within three years the Dow had instead fallen toward 7,000. It finally crossed 36,000 in November 2021 — about 22 years later — and, as reviewers noted, for reasons unrelated to the book's theory. Anyone who acted on "a few years" lived through two crashes first.

The needle, on the day: the mechanism was a real academic puzzle honestly argued — SUPPORTED as a possibility. The timeline ("next few years") and the certainty were UNVERIFIED dressed as arrival time. The lesson here is the sneakiest one on this page: eventually right is not right. A prediction without a deadline can never be wrong — which is exactly why our flight simulator's resolution rule has a fixed window, written in advance.

Bearish call · early by years = wrong in leverage-time
The warning at Dow 6,437
In December 1996, two of the most respected market researchers alive warned the Federal Reserve that the stock market was poised for a big fall.
Robert Shiller (Yale) & John Campbell (Harvard), December 1996, Dow ≈ 6,437[18] — as recorded in a contemporaneous Harvard economist's account.
Reality: the market roughly doubled over the following three years before the 2000 crash came — and even that crash never brought the Dow back down to 6,437. Shiller was later proven deeply right about the bubble's psychology (and won a Nobel Prize) — and anyone who bet money on the 1996 warning with borrowed size was wiped out by the rally before being vindicated.

The needle, on the day: grounded in real valuation evidence — SUPPORTED as a caution. This entry exists because of what it teaches about time: in leveraged money, early and wrong are the same word. Being right about the destination doesn't save you if the road first runs the other way farther than your cushion reaches. This is the bear-side mirror of Dow 36,000, and together they're the record's spine: neither side owns wrongness.

Bears · wrong hundreds of times (so far)
The obituaries of Bitcoin
Since 2010, commentators, economists, and headlines have publicly declared Bitcoin dead, dying, or going to zero — a popular tracker has catalogued hundreds[19] of such on-record declarations.
An aggregate entry — no single author. The tally comes from a well-known community-run "Bitcoin obituaries" tracker; we cite it as a popular count, not a study.
Reality: through every declared death, Bitcoin went on to trade at prices its obituary-writers called impossible. The honest other half: this entry does NOT mean Bitcoin can't fail, and it sits in the same museum wing as Terra — where "it always comes back" was the confident story right up until it didn't. Past survival is evidence of past survival, nothing more.

The needle, on the days: most obituaries were UNSUPPORTED CONCLUSIONS — "this will die" is the same all-future-time claim as Fisher's "permanently," pointed the other way. The pair belongs together on purpose: "forever" and "never" are the two words the needle grades hardest, in either direction.

How to read any prediction while its author is still alive (four questions, no expert needed):
1. Is there a deadline? No deadline = unfalsifiable = not a prediction, just a mood. (Dow 36,000's real error was "a few years.")
2. Does it contain "forever" words? "Permanently," "never," "always," "dead" — all-future-time claims from finite past data. The needle's hardest grades, both directions.
3. Who gets paid if you believe it? Not proof of anything — but the brokerage predicting a doubling, and the pundit selling the crash course, both earn on your belief, not their accuracy.
4. What did this person's LAST ten predictions do? Almost nobody checks, which is why almost nobody has to be right. A record exists so somebody keeps score.
